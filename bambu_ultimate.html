Understanding Bambu Studio & Bambu Lab Printers
What the ecosystem is, which printer is which, and how to get started fast
What is Bambu Studio?

Bambu Studio is the official slicer for all Bambu Lab printers. It is based on the PrusaSlicer / OrcaSlicer open-source codebase but heavily customized and optimized for the Bambu ecosystem — cloud integration, AMS management, AI features, and one-click send-to-printer workflows.

Bambu Studio: Official, tight Bambu integration, cloud slicing, simpler UI. Best if you only own Bambu printers.
OrcaSlicer: Community fork with more calibration tools, supports all brands, more advanced settings. Great for power users.

5 Things To Do First (New Bambu Owner)
1
Update firmware — Settings → Firmware Update. Do printer, then AMS, then toolhead in sequence.
2
Calibrate first layer — Run the built-in first layer calibration, adjust Live Z until lines squish perfectly with no gaps or over-squish.
3
Run filament calibration — Use Bambu-branded filament first, let the printer auto-calibrate flow and PA values.
4
Explore Makerworld — Browse models in the Bambu Studio sidebar. Print a sample that showcases AMS or speed.
5
Try AMS with 2 colors — Load two filaments in AMS slots 1 and 2, find a simple two-color model, and run your first multi-color print.
Key FDM concepts apply here too: layers, infill, walls, supports, and bed adhesion work the same as any FDM slicer — Bambu just automates many of the calibration steps that used to be manual.
AMS — Automatic Material System (Complete Guide)
Everything you need to know about multi-color and multi-material printing with Bambu's AMS
AMS System Overview

The AMS (Automatic Material System) holds 4 spools and handles automatic loading, unloading, and switching between filaments during a print. No manual swaps needed.

  • AMS (Standard): Works with X1C, P1S, P1P — connects via PTFE tube to toolhead, sits on top of/beside printer
  • AMS Lite: A1/A1 Mini variant — external, 4-spool, simplified mechanism
  • AMS Hub: Connects up to 4 AMS units = 16 filaments total simultaneously
RFID Tags: Bambu-branded filaments have RFID chips — brand, color, material type, and remaining length auto-detected. Third-party spools = enter manually or use generic profiles.
Humidity: AMS has a built-in humidity sensor. If reading is above 45% RH, add desiccant or run the AMS dehumidify mode to protect filament quality.
Multi-Color Workflow (Step by Step)
1
Import your model — Use a multi-body STL, 3MF with pre-assigned colors, or a single-color model you'll paint manually.
2
Assign filament slots — In the right panel (Filament tab), drag filament slots to each object/part, or use the dropdown to assign which AMS slot prints which part.
3
Review the Wipe Tower — Auto-generated to purge filament between color changes. Reposition it on the plate, adjust size if needed. Larger = cleaner transitions but more waste.
4
Set Flush Volumes — Controls how much filament purges per color switch. Critical for clean color changes (see table below).
5
Optional: Flush into Infill — Enable this to purge waste filament into the model's infill rather than the wipe tower. Saves material but adds print time.
6
Slice and Send — Slice normally, preview color changes in layer view, then send to printer.
Flush Volume Reference
Transition TypeFlush VolumeWhy
Dark → Light200–500 mm³Dark pigment stains strongly — needs aggressive purging
Dark → Dark100–200 mm³Moderate — different dark colors still bleed
Light → Light50–100 mm³Minimal — light colors mix less visibly
Light → Dark50–100 mm³Dark covers light — least purging needed
Pro tip: Order your color changes so you go light → dark where possible to minimize wipe tower waste. Bambu Studio can auto-optimize color order.

AMS Loading & Calibration

Loading Filament

  1. Open AMS cover, place spool in slot
  2. Feed filament end into the AMS inlet until it catches
  3. On printer screen: Settings → AMS → Load
  4. Tap the slot, then tap Load — printer feeds filament to toolhead
  5. Wait for confirmation on screen

Unloading Filament

  1. On printer screen: tap the AMS slot you want to unload
  2. Tap Unload
  3. Wait for retraction — filament pulls back into AMS
  4. Remove spool when retraction is complete

Filament Cutting (X1C / P1 Series)

  • X1C and P1 series have a built-in cutter at the toolhead
  • Cuts filament clean during swaps — no ooze, no drag-out artifacts
  • A1 / AMS Lite uses retraction + tip-forming instead of cutting
  • Cutter blade can wear — inspect periodically, replaceable

AMS Hub (Multi-AMS Setup)

  • Daisy-chain up to 4 AMS units via hub connector
  • Each AMS adds 4 slots = 16 total filaments
  • Update firmware on every AMS unit individually
  • Assign slots 1–16 in Bambu Studio filament panel

Infill Patterns & Density

Pattern Selection

PatternStrengthSpeedBest For
Gyroid ⭐Excellent (all axes)GoodAll-around best — functional parts
GridGoodFastSimple functional parts
HoneycombGood (vertical)ModerateVertical load-bearing
LightningMinimalFastestDisplay models, weight saving only

Density Guide

  • 5–10%: Display models, minimum material
  • 15% ⭐: Standard — most decorative/light-duty prints
  • 25%: Functional parts, some stress
  • 40%+: Structural, high-stress, mechanical
  • 80–100%: Maximum strength (rarely needed — walls are more efficient)
Wall vs Infill strength: For strong parts, add more wall loops before increasing infill. 4 walls + 15% infill > 2 walls + 50% infill.
Top / Bottom Settings

Shell Count

  • 3 layers: Minimum functional (may show infill pattern)
  • 4 layers ⭐: Standard — solid top/bottom surface
  • 6 layers: Strong / watertight / dimensional accuracy critical

Top Fill Pattern

  • Monotonic ⭐: Lines always in same direction — smoothest visual result
  • Concentric: Spiral outward — interesting look, less smooth
  • Grid: Cross-hatch — fastest but shows lines more

Ironing

Ironing ON: After printing top surface, nozzle does a second slow pass re-melting the top layer to glass-smooth finish. Adds time but excellent for lids, nameplates, anything on display. Enable per-object in Bambu Studio.

Detect Thin Walls

Enable this for models with fine features thinner than your nozzle diameter. Without it, thin features may be skipped entirely by the slicer.
Strength Tips Specific to Bambu
  • Trust the stock profiles: Bambu's built-in presets (0.20mm Standard, etc.) are well-tuned by Bambu Lab engineers — don't over-tweak if prints look good already.
  • Strength priority: Wall count > Infill density > Layer height. Add walls first.
  • Best functional part recipe: 4 walls + 25% Gyroid + 0.20mm + 4 top/bottom shells
  • Weak layer bonding: Lower print speed or raise nozzle temperature by 5°C to improve layer adhesion for structural parts.
  • Orientation matters most: Z-axis (layer stacking direction) is always weakest. Orient parts so stress runs along X/Y, not pulling layers apart.
Don't do this: 2 walls + 50% grid infill. It sounds "strong" from high infill but walls carry most structural load. This combo is weaker AND slower than 4 walls + 25% gyroid.
Supports & Bed Adhesion
When and how to use supports, brim/raft, and getting that perfect first layer
Support Types

Tree Supports ⭐ (Recommended)

  • Branches grow up from bed to contact points only
  • Less material used overall
  • Much easier to remove — snap off cleanly
  • Better overhang surface quality
  • Best for: organic shapes, figures, curved overhangs
  • Slows slicer computation for complex models

Normal Supports

  • Solid column grid under all overhangs
  • Faster to slice
  • Better for large flat overhangs and wide bridge spans
  • More material waste
  • Can be harder to remove from tight spaces
  • Best for: mechanical parts, flat overhangs, holes
Key Support Settings
SettingRecommended ValueEffect
Overhang Threshold45°Auto-generates support for anything steeper than 45° from vertical
Top Z Distance0.2 mmGap between support top and part underside — smaller = better surface, harder removal
Bottom Z Distance0.2 mmGap between bed/part and where support starts growing
Interface Layers2–4 layersSmooth layers at top of support — dramatically improves overhang surface quality
Interface PatternGrid or ConcentricGrid = stronger interface, Concentric = often easier removal
Support Wall Loops0 (easy removal) or 1–2 (strong)0 = breaks away easily, 1–2 = more structural support column
Interface Layers tip: Setting 2–4 interface layers with a 0.2mm Z gap gives a noticeably smoother underside surface compared to no interface — always use this for visible overhangs.
Support Painting

Manually control exactly where supports are generated using the Support Painting tool in the top toolbar.

Blue paint: Enforce support — force support generation on this face even if angle doesn't require it.
Red paint: Block support — prevent support on this face even if auto-detection would add it.
  • Brush size is adjustable for precision
  • Use red blocking to prevent supports inside tunnels or cavities that don't need them
  • Use blue enforcement for tricky low-angle overhangs that auto-detect misses
  • Works the same as OrcaSlicer's support painting
Support Removal Tips
  • Needle-nose pliers: Grip support column base and twist/rock to snap free — most effective technique
  • Flush cutters: Snip support columns close to the model surface, then peel remaining nubs
  • Dental pick / hobby knife: Work out embedded supports from tight cavities and internal spaces
  • Cool the print first: Supports release much more easily when the print has fully cooled to room temperature
  • Bambu Support W: Water-soluble support material for PLA — dissolves in water, no manual removal needed

First Layer — The Most Important Layer

Live Z Adjustment

  • During the first layer, tap +0.05 mm to raise nozzle (less squish)
  • Tap −0.05 mm to lower nozzle (more squish)
  • Adjust in real-time until first layer looks right
  • Saved as Baby Step Z offset — persists for future prints

Ideal First Layer Appearance

  • Lines are slightly squished — visible flattening
  • No gaps between adjacent lines
  • No lifting at corners or edges
  • Not so squished that nozzle drags through material

Bambu Auto-Leveling

X1C Lidar: Scans the entire bed surface with lidar before every print to create a compensation mesh — handles warped PEI sheets automatically.
AI First Layer Inspection (X1C): Camera watches the first layer in real-time. If it detects adhesion failure, under-extrusion, or lifting, it pauses and alerts you via Bambu Handy app.
P1 / A1: Vibration-based auto bed leveling — slower but still very effective. Probes a grid of points and compensates mesh.

Firmware Updates

How to Update

  1. In Bambu Studio: Settings → Firmware Update
  2. Or on printer screen: Settings → About → Firmware
  3. Update sequence: Printer first → AMS → Toolhead
  4. Wait for each step to complete before proceeding
  5. Printer restarts automatically after update

Update Best Practices

Auto-update ON: Recommended for most users — Bambu releases frequent improvements including speed optimizations, new material profiles, and bug fixes.
Production workflow: Read release notes before updating. Occasionally an update changes default calibration values. Schedule updates during downtime, not mid-project.

PA / Nylon (X1C / P1S)

  • PA6-CF, PA12-CF: 280°C nozzle, 90°C bed, 0% fan
  • Engineering nozzle (0.4mm hardened steel) required
  • Must dry 12–16 hours at 70°C before printing
  • Print from a dry box if possible — PA absorbs moisture from air during printing
PA is highly hygroscopic. Wet filament will cause bubbling, poor layer adhesion, and failed prints. Never skip drying.

PC — Polycarbonate (X1C with Hardened Nozzle)

  • 280–310°C nozzle, 100–120°C bed, 0% fan
  • All-metal hotend required
  • Engineering plate recommended
  • Hardest material to print reliably
Start with a PC-ABS blend for a more forgiving introduction to polycarbonate printing.

Speed Optimization for Bambu

Bambu Speed Architecture

Bambu printers are engineered for high speed by default. Avoid slowing everything down unnecessarily — the hardware and firmware are tuned to handle fast motion safely.
Setting X1C / P1S A1 / A1 Mini
Outer Wall150 mm/s100 mm/s
Inner Wall500 mm/s350 mm/s
Infill500 mm/s350 mm/s
Travel700 mm/s500 mm/s

Built-In Speed Profiles

Silent~50 mm/s outer wall — quieter, ideal for overnight prints
Normal~100 mm/s outer wall — balanced quality and speed
Sport~150 mm/s outer wall — fast with acceptable quality
Ludicrous~200–250 mm/s outer wall — draft use only, decreased quality

What Affects Speed Choice

  • Outer wall speed — most visible quality impact. Do not exceed 150 mm/s for standard quality output.
  • Infill speed — hidden inside, can be maximized (300–500 mm/s safely).
  • Layer cooling — at very high speeds, layers may not cool before the next is deposited. Increase fan speed or reduce print speed.
  • Tall thin prints — reduce speed by 50% to prevent resonance oscillation knocking the print off the bed.

Input Shaping (Built into Bambu Firmware)

  • Factory calibrated and also supports user recalibration
  • Eliminates ringing and ghosting artifacts at high speeds
  • Bambu's implementation is one of the best in consumer FDM — key competitive advantage
  • Recalibrate: Monthly, or when significant weight is added to the toolhead

Troubleshooting Bambu-Specific Issues

AMS Jam

  • Dirty tip cut: Trim the filament tip at 45° with flush cutters before loading into AMS
  • Humidity jam: Brittle filament that snaps in the AMS is wet — dry it before use
  • Buffer backup: Check that the AMS buffer tube near the hub isn't kinked
  • Reload cycle: Tap the AMS slot in the app → Load to re-trigger the loading sequence
  • Manual bypass: Turn off AMS in settings, load direct from external spool to extruder

First Layer Fail (with Lidar / AI Inspection)

  • "Abnormal first layer" alert on X1C means the camera detected an adhesion issue
  • Check: Z offset (live adjust ±0.05mm), clean build plate, correct plate type for filament
Disable AI Check: Settings → Print → Disable First Layer Inspection. Use this for textured or dark-colored filaments that confuse the AI camera.

Nozzle Clog

SymptomsUnder-extrusion, clicking extruder, no filament flow, grinding marks on filament
  • Quick purge: Maintenance → Extrude/Retract 50–100mm to diagnose flow
  • Cold pull: Heat to print temp, retract slowly, cool to 90°C, pull fast and firm
  • Nozzle swap: Bambu nozzles are hot-swap. Preheat to 200°C, spin off with wrench, spin new one on.
Nozzle Use Case
0.4mm BrassStandard — PLA, PETG, ABS
0.4mm Hardened SteelAbrasive filaments — CF, PA, PC
0.2mm BrassFine detail prints
0.6mm / 0.8mm BrassFast/draft prints, large models

  • Send to printer fails: Check LAN cable if wired; switch to LAN mode instead of cloud mode

Advanced Workflows

Sequential Printing (By Object)

Prints objects one at a time — completes each fully before moving to the next.

  • Advantage: No stringing between objects; easy to cancel mid-plate without losing completed parts
  • Requirement: Objects must be shorter than the toolhead clearance height
  • Objects must have clearance between them (slicer checks automatically)
Process→ Others → Print Sequence → By Object

Support Painting Workflow

  1. Import model and enable auto-supports first as a baseline
  2. Click Support Painting in the toolbar
  3. Red-brush: remove auto-supports from cosmetic/visible faces
  4. Blue-brush: add support only on critical overhang faces
  5. Tune: adjust angle threshold per painted region
  6. Result: much cleaner final print with minimal removal work

Modifier Mesh Workflow

  1. Right-click object in list → Add Modifier → Box / Cylinder / Sphere
  2. Resize modifier to cover the area needing different settings
  3. In modifier settings: override infill %, wall count, or layer height
  4. Use case: Boss holes at 80% infill; surrounding area at 15%
  5. Modifier meshes are saved inside .3mf project files

Combining Objects and Assemblies

  • Import multiple STLs — position relative to each other on the plate
  • Assemble mode: lock relative positions of objects
  • Save as .3mf to preserve positions, settings, and filament assignments
  • Good for interlocking parts and assemblies that print together

Hollow Models with Drain Holes

Process→ Others → Enable Hollow → set wall thickness
  • Add drain holes: click "Add hole" → position on model surface
  • Useful for reducing weight on large decorative FDM prints

Per-Object Settings Workflow

  1. Select object in viewport or object list
  2. Right-click → Object Settings
  3. Override: layer height, infill pattern, or wall count for this object only
  4. Ideal for multi-object plates where one part needs a different quality level

Text Embossing / Engraving

TText tool — click on a model face to place text
  • Set font, size, and depth: positive value = emboss, negative = engrave
  • FDM emboss minimum: 0.5mm height, 1.5mm minimum feature size
  • FDM engrave minimum: 0.3mm deep, 0.5mm wide

Practice Projects for Bambu Printers

Project 1 — Multicolor Nameplate (AMS / Filament Swap)

Skills: AMS workflow, color assignment, text embossing

  1. Create a rectangular base (100×40×5mm) in Tinkercad or download a flat name sign STL
  2. Import into Bambu Studio
  3. Use the Text tool: emboss your name on the surface (3mm height, sans-serif font)
  4. With AMS: assign base to filament 1, raised letters to filament 2 (split by height automatically)
  5. Single extruder: add a color change pause at the layer where letters begin
  6. Assign dark color to base, bright contrasting color to letters
  7. Settings: 0.1mm layers, 4 walls, 15% infill, ironing ON for base surface
  8. Slice and verify color split in preview before printing
  9. AMS: fully automatic. Single extruder: pause at color change layer, swap filament, resume.

Project 2 — Lithophane (Photo Print)

Skills: Model import, translucent filament, fine detail settings

  1. Go to lithophanemaker.com or itslitho.com
  2. Upload photo, generate STL lithophane (flat or curved option)
  3. Import into Bambu Studio
  4. Material: white PLA — natural/translucent works best for light transmission
  5. Settings: 0.1mm layers, 3 walls, 100% infill, no top/bottom shells (infill IS the surface)
  6. Orientation: vertical (standing up) for best detail; horizontal is faster but less sharp
  7. No supports needed when printing vertically
  8. Print time: 2–5 hours depending on size
  9. Hold finished print up to a light source to reveal the photo
  10. Optional: print a simple frame STL in a contrasting color

Project 3 — Functional Enclosure Print (ABS / ASA)

Skills: Enclosed printer use, engineering material settings, warping prevention

  1. Download an electronics enclosure STL (Raspberry Pi case or similar)
  2. Material: ASA (outdoor / heat-resistant) or ABS
  3. Printer required: X1C or P1S (must be enclosed)
  4. Settings: 0.2mm layers, 4 walls, 25% gyroid infill, 3 top/bottom shells
  5. Close all doors and top panel; warm the enclosure before starting
  6. Bed: high-temp plate, 90°C, glue stick applied
  7. Fan: 0% — critical for ABS/ASA (fan causes warping and layer splitting)
  8. Brim: 5mm
  9. Monitor first layer: should adhere fully with no lifting corners
  10. After print: let cool in closed enclosure for 30 minutes before removing (no drafts)
Never open the enclosure during an ABS/ASA print. Sudden temperature drop will cause the part to warp or crack.

Project 4 — Rapid Prototype Phone Case (TPU)

Skills: High speed printing, TPU flexible material

  1. Download phone case STL for your model (Printables has thousands of options)
  2. Material: Bambu TPU 95A or 3rd party TPU (use external spool)
  3. Settings: 0.2mm layers, 3 walls, 15% gyroid infill
  4. Speed: max 50mm/s outer wall, 100mm/s inner — do NOT use Bambu's normal high-speed presets with TPU
  5. Temperature: 230°C nozzle, 40°C bed
  6. Supports: usually not needed for phone case geometry
  7. Retraction: 0.5mm (direct drive Bambu) — too much retraction jams TPU
  8. Fan: 50%
  9. Disable AMS; load TPU directly from external spool
  10. Result: flexible, functional phone case in 1–2 hours

Project 5 — Speed Benchmark (Benchy at Ludicrous Speed)

Skills: High-speed mode, quality trade-offs, printer capability testing

  1. Download 3DBenchy (standard FDM test model — benchie.org)
  2. Import at standard size — do not scale (60×31×48mm)
  3. Settings: Ludicrous preset, or manually set outer wall 200mm/s, inner/infill 500mm/s
  4. 0.2mm layers, standard settings otherwise
  5. Print — expect 10–20 minutes depending on printer model
  6. Examine: chimney (overhangs), cabin windows (detail), hull (stringing), lettering
  7. Print a second Benchy at Normal mode for direct comparison
  8. Photograph both side by side — documents actual quality/speed trade-off for your printer
  9. Find your optimal speed — usually between Normal and Ludicrous for daily-use prints

Project 6 — Assembly Print (Multi-Part with AMS)

Skills: Multi-file project, AMS multi-color, assembly management, tolerances

  1. Download a multi-part model (mechanical pencil, fidget cube, or similar from Makerworld/Printables)
  2. Import ALL parts into Bambu Studio as separate objects
  3. Assign different filament colors to different parts via the right panel
  4. Arrange on plate: flat faces down, check all orientations
  5. Add supports where needed using support painting
  6. Settings: 0.2mm standard, 3 walls, 20% infill
  7. After printing: remove supports and assemble — should click or slide together as designed
  8. Fit too tight: scale specific part to 100.5% and reprint
  9. Fit too loose: scale to 99.5% and reprint
  10. Note: FDM tolerances are typically ±0.2mm — choose or design models that account for this

Long-Term Printer Care

X/Y RailsLubricate every 200 hours with Bambu grease or Super Lube 21030, or when squeaking
Z ScrewApply PTFE dry lubricant or light machine oil periodically
Belt TensionPluck X/Y belts — should sound like a low guitar string. Not slack, not overly tight.
Camera LensClean monthly with microfiber cloth — dust degrades AI inspection accuracy
Keep a spare hotend replacement kit stocked: extra nozzles (brass + hardened steel), PTFE tube, and thermistor. These are the parts most likely to need replacement during extended printing.
Hotend PTFE tube: Replace when grinding or clogging becomes frequent. A degraded PTFE tube is a common hidden cause of persistent jams.
