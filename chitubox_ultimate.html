Understanding Resin 3D Printing & Chitubox

How MSLA printing works, the Elegoo lineup, safety essentials, and the complete workflow from STL to finished print.

How MSLA / LCD Resin Printing Works

MSLA (Masked Stereolithography) uses an LCD screen as a photomask. A UV light source behind the screen shines through transparent pixels, curing liquid photopolymer resin in the shape of each layer.

  • The build plate is at the top and lifts upward — the print hangs inverted
  • Liquid resin sits in a tank with a transparent FEP film at the bottom
  • Each layer: UV fires through FEP → cures resin → plate lifts → layer peels from FEP → new resin floods in → repeat
  • Resolution is determined by: screen pixels ÷ print area = XY accuracy (µm per pixel)
Key insight: In the Chitubox viewport, what looks like the "bottom" of your model actually prints FIRST and sticks to the build plate. The "top" finishes last, hanging in mid-air.

Key Resin Printing Concepts
  1. Exposure time: How long UV light fires per layer. Over-expose = brittle, blurry, elephant foot. Under-expose = failed/crumbly print.
  2. Bottom layers: First 4–10 layers receive much longer exposure (30–80s) to bond firmly to the build plate.
  3. Suction forces: Closed-bottom models create a vacuum cup on the FEP during peel. This tears supports or rips the layer entirely.
  4. FEP film: Clear membrane at tank bottom. Wears out and becomes cloudy. Replace every 3–6 months or when scratched/cloudy.
  5. Support contact point: Tiny tip that touches your model. Too thick = noticeable scar. Too thin = support snaps mid-print = failure.
  6. Z offset: How close the build plate is to the FEP at layer zero. Critical: too far = no adhesion. Too close = FEP damage.
  7. Anti-aliasing: Softens the jagged pixel-grid edges on curves for smoother surface quality.
  8. Lift speed: How fast the plate rises after each layer. Too fast = FEP stress, torn layers, failed prints.
⚠ Resin Safety — CRITICAL
Uncured resin is toxic and a skin/eye irritant. Do NOT handle with bare hands. Do NOT breathe vapors.
  • Always wear nitrile gloves — not latex (latex degrades with resin solvents)
  • Eye protection when decanting or washing
  • Ventilate your space: open window, use exhaust fan, or activated carbon air filtration
  • Waste resin disposal: cure all liquid resin waste with sunlight or UV lamp until solid, then dispose as solid waste. NEVER pour liquid resin down the drain.
  • IPA wash waste: let IPA evaporate (outdoors) or cure the resin out with UV then filter and reuse
  • Skin contact: wash immediately with soap and water. Avoid alcohol — it increases absorption. Seek medical attention for eye contact.
  • Keep resin away from children and pets. Store in dark bottles away from UV.
Some resins (especially water-washable) have stronger odors. Always ventilate regardless of resin type.
Complete Print Workflow — STL to Finished Model
  1. Design / Download STL — Create in CAD (Fusion 360, Blender) or download from Thingiverse, Printables, MyMiniFactory
  2. Import to Chitubox — File → Open or drag-and-drop .STL/.OBJ/.3MF file
  3. Orient the model — Tilt 15–45° to minimize flat suction areas and place cosmetic faces away from supports
  4. Add Supports — Auto-support first, then manually fix gaps. Check for unsupported islands.
  5. Hollow (optional) — For large models: hollow with 2mm walls + add 2 drain holes to save resin
  6. Set Print Settings — Configure exposure, layer height, lift speed for your printer + resin combination
  7. Slice — Click Slice. Review the layer-by-layer preview for islands or gaps.
  8. Export .ctb file — Save to USB drive or send to printer via network/app
  9. Level build plate — Only needed periodically or after moving printer
  10. Print — Monitor first few layers, check printer display for layer previews
  11. Wash — IPA (isopropyl alcohol) or dedicated wash solution (Elegoo Wash & Cure). 3–5 minutes agitation.
  12. Cure — UV curing station (Elegoo Mercury Plus or similar). 2–5 min per side depending on resin.
  13. Post-process — Remove supports, sand if needed, prime and paint

Intermediate

Print Settings — Exposure & Layers

The most critical settings in resin printing. Dial these in and 90% of print failures disappear.

Golden Rule: Always test a new resin with a calibration print (Ameralabs Town, Cones of Calibration, or RERF test) before printing expensive models. One 20-minute test saves hours of failed prints.
Exposure Settings — Most Critical

Normal Exposure Time (per layer)

How long the UV light fires for each normal layer. This is your primary tuning knob.

Resin TypeTypical RangeNotes
Standard PLA-like2.0 – 4.0 sStart at 2.5s, test up/down
ABS-like / tough2.5 – 4.0 sHarder to cure, needs slightly more
Water-washable2.0 – 3.0 sOften more sensitive, start low
Flexible / elastic3.0 – 5.0 sNeeds longer cure for flex properties
High-speed resins1.0 – 2.0 sOptimized for fast printing
8K/12K/16K screens have lower per-pixel light intensity than older 2K/4K screens. You may need slightly longer exposure times (+0.5–1s) than older community recommendations suggest.
Over-exposed: Loss of fine detail, blurry edges, elephant foot, brittle prints, parts fused together.
Under-exposed: Layers delaminate, soft/tacky surface, print fails mid-way, parts peel off plate.

Bottom Exposure Time

The first N layers (defined by Bottom Layer Count) use this longer exposure to ensure strong adhesion to the build plate.

Resin TypeTypical Range
Standard resins30 – 60 seconds
Water-washable40 – 80 seconds
ABS-like35 – 65 seconds
Too short = plate adhesion failure (print falls mid-print). Too long = severe elephant foot and very difficult plate removal.

Bottom Layer Count

How many layers use the bottom exposure time.

  • 4 layers: Minimum, faster start, less elephant foot
  • 6 layers: Standard — good balance
  • 8–10 layers: Maximum plate grip, harder removal

Transition Layers

Gradual step-down from bottom exposure to normal exposure. Reduces elephant foot significantly.

  • 0: Abrupt change (older setting)
  • 4–8: Recommended — smooth transition
  • 10+: Very gentle, minimal elephant foot

Anti-Aliasing & Image Processing

Anti-Aliasing Level

Smooths the jagged pixel-grid steps on curved and diagonal surfaces.

  • Off: Raw pixels — visible stepping on curves
  • 2x: Recommended — noticeable improvement
  • 4x: Better, slight performance cost
  • 8x: Diminishing returns on most resins

Grey Level & Image Blur

Grey level: Edge pixels are partially cured (not full white, not black) for smoother transitions at layer boundaries.

Image blur: Applies a slight blur to slice images — reduces visible layer lines. Level 1–2 recommended. Too much = loss of fine detail.

Elephant Foot Compensation

Elephant foot is the flared, spread-out base that occurs when bottom layers are over-exposed. UV bleeds outward at the first layers, making the base wider than the rest of the model.

Causes

  • Bottom exposure time too long
  • Bottom layer count too high
  • No transition layers
  • Model printed flat on plate (no raft)

Fixes

  • Elephant foot compensation: +0.1 to +0.2mm (shrinks bottom layers)
  • Add 4–8 transition layers
  • Reduce bottom exposure by 5–10s
  • Tilt model off plate (use supports)
Elephant foot compensation in Chitubox shrinks each bottom layer by the specified amount to pre-compensate for UV bleed. Start at +0.15mm and adjust.

Supports — Complete Mastery

Supports are the single most common cause of print success or failure. Master this and your success rate jumps dramatically.

Why this matters more in resin than FDM: Every single layer must successfully peel from the FEP. An unsupported area experiences full peel forces with nothing holding it in place. Result: the layer tears, the print fails catastrophically, and you have a blob of partially cured resin floating in your tank.
Support Types
TypeTip DiameterColumn WidthBest ForScar Level
Light0.2 – 0.3mmThinDelicate surfaces, small details, facesMinimal
Medium0.3 – 0.4mmMidGeneral use — most supportsModerate
Heavy0.4 – 0.6mmThickLarge flat areas, high-suction zones, heavy partsNoticeable
TreeVariableBranching trunkDense areas, uses less resinVariable
Smaller tip = less scar but higher risk of support failure. Larger tip = more reliable but leaves a visible mark. Match support type to the importance of the surface.
Key Support Parameters — Explained
ParameterDescriptionRecommended Values
Contact Diameter (tip) The tiny point that touches your model surface. Smaller = less scar, higher failure risk. Light: 0.3mm | Medium: 0.4mm | Heavy: 0.5mm
Contact Depth How deep the tip penetrates into the model surface. Deeper = stronger grip, more scar. 0.2 – 0.3mm typical
Upper Diameter Width of the support column just below the contact point. Wider = more rigid support. 1.5 – 2.0mm
Lower Diameter Base width of the support column where it meets the raft/plate. Wider = more stable. 2.0 – 3.0mm
Support Density For auto-supports: how close together placements are. Higher density = more supports = more reliable but more cleanup. Start at Medium, increase for large flat overhangs
Top Connection Angle Surface angle threshold below which supports are automatically added. 45° = standard. Lower = more aggressive support. 45° default (adjust to 40° for tricky models)
Auto-Support Workflow — Step by Step
  1. Orient the model first. Supports are placed relative to orientation. Always orient BEFORE adding supports.
  2. Click Support in the left toolbar to enter support mode
  3. Click Auto-Support — choose density (start with Medium)
  4. Preview the result: Red-highlighted areas = still needs support. Grey/green = supported.
  5. Switch to "+" mode to manually add supports — click on any unsupported face
  6. Switch to "-" mode to remove any auto-placed supports that hit cosmetic surfaces you care about
  7. Rotate the model 360° in the viewport to inspect from all angles. No large unsupported spans.
  8. Key rule: No more than 5mm of unsupported overhang in any horizontal direction
After auto-support, always manually inspect and adjust. Auto-support is a starting point, not a final answer. 5 minutes of manual cleanup prevents most failures.
Critical Areas — Always Check These
  • Lowest point of each feature: The first layer any feature appears needs support underneath it
  • Horizontal "ceiling" faces: Fully horizontal overhangs (0° from flat) are the hardest to print — need heavy support
  • Large flat areas parallel to FEP: Act like suction cups — need heavy supports AND consider tilting the model
  • Thin protrusions: Arms, fingers, antennae, horns — support the tips, not just the base
  • Islands in slice preview: Any layer cross-section that floats disconnected = guaranteed failure point
  • Hollow cavity openings: Edges around drain holes need support from below
After auto-support, slice the model and drag through the layer preview. Look for any yellow-highlighted islands. Every island needs a support added.
Raft / Base Settings

What a Raft Does

  • Creates a flat platform across all support bases
  • Improves plate adhesion — all supports connect via raft to plate
  • Makes removal from build plate easier (pry under raft)
  • Distributes peel forces across larger area

Raft Settings

  • Thickness: 1–2mm. Thicker = stronger but harder to remove.
  • Area: Auto-set to cover all support footprints. Extend slightly if needed.
  • Taper: Some rafts have tapered edges for easier removal.
Always use a raft. The minor resin cost is worth the major reliability improvement.
Removing Supports — Best Practices
  1. Remove supports BEFORE UV curing if possible — supports snap off cleanly when resin is in a semi-cured "green state"
  2. Use flush cutters to clip at the base of each support column
  3. Grip the support near the model contact point to avoid tearing the surface
  4. For stubborn supports: use a dental pick or hobby knife to pry tip from surface
  5. Sand scar marks with 400–800 grit wet/dry sandpaper, then prime
Removing supports AFTER full UV cure is much harder — they become brittle and the contact point is strongly bonded. The preferred order is: print → wash → remove supports → cure.
Intermediate

Hollowing & Drain Holes

Save 60–80% of your resin costs with proper hollowing. But skip the drain holes and your print explodes during curing.

Why Hollow Your Prints

Resin Savings

  • 100mm figurine solid: 100–300ml resin
  • Same figurine hollowed (2mm walls): 20–50ml resin
  • Savings: 60–80% of resin cost
  • Faster print time (fewer solid layers to cure)

Other Benefits

  • Lighter finished model
  • Less suction force from the model body
  • Reduced warping stress during cure
  • Easier to cure all the way through (thick solid = interior may stay uncured)
Critical caveat: Hollow models REQUIRE drain holes. Without them, trapped liquid resin expands during UV curing and cracks or shatters your print. No exceptions.
Hollowing in Chitubox — Step by Step
  1. Select your model in the viewport
  2. Click Hollow in the left toolbar (or Edit menu → Hollow)
  3. Set Wall Thickness: 1.5mm thin / 2.0mm standard / 3.0mm strong
  4. Set Infill Type: None (display models) / Grid / Triangle (structural models)
  5. Check "Close inner holes" if your model has surface openings you want sealed
  6. Switch to X-ray view to preview the hollow cavity inside
  7. Click "Hollow" (or Apply) to execute. Model updates in viewport.
After hollowing, check the X-ray view carefully. Thin wall sections (where geometry is complex) may have gaps — these create weak points. Increase wall thickness if you see thin spots.
Wall Thickness Guide by Use Case
ThicknessUse CaseNotes
1.5mmSmall figurines, display items handled gentlyFragile — handle carefully. Keep under 80mm tall.
2.0mmStandard figurines and models under 100mmMost common choice. Good balance of savings and strength.
2.5mmLarger models, items that get regular handlingNoticeably more robust. Good for models over 100mm.
3.0mmFunctional parts, models over 150mm, mechanical stressNear-solid strength in most areas. Modest resin savings.
Solid (no hollow)Models under 30mm, jewelry, connectors, load-bearing partsSkip hollowing entirely — resin cost is low and strength is maximized.
⚠ Drain Holes — Non-Negotiable
NEVER print a hollow model without drain holes. Trapped liquid resin creates hydrostatic pressure during UV curing as it expands. Result: cracks, delamination, or catastrophic print failure. Always add at least 2 drain holes.

Why Two Holes?

Think of tipping a water bottle with your finger over the opening — it barely drains. Two holes: one lets resin out, one lets air in. Same principle.

  • Drain hole: Place at the lowest point of the hollow cavity in print orientation — liquid resin pools here and needs to exit during washing
  • Air vent hole: Place at the highest point or opposite side — allows air to enter as resin drains out, preventing vacuum lock

Drain Hole Sizing

Model SizeHole DiameterNotes
Small (<50mm)2.5 – 3.0mmMinimum to allow resin flow + IPA to wash inside
Medium (50–100mm)3.0 – 4.0mmStandard. Allows cleaning brush or syringe access.
Large (>100mm)4.0 – 6.0mmLarger cavity needs larger holes for complete drainage

Adding Drain Holes in Chitubox

  1. Select your (already-hollowed) model
  2. Click the Hole tool in the left toolbar
  3. Click on the model surface where you want the first hole (lowest print point)
  4. Set diameter (3–5mm) and depth (must be greater than wall thickness)
  5. Confirm placement — hole appears as a circular marker
  6. Repeat for the second hole at the highest/opposite point
  7. Switch to X-ray view to verify holes penetrate completely through the shell
Verify drain holes in the layer preview slider too — the hole should appear as a gap in the cross-section at those layer heights. If you see closed layers where holes should be, they didn't penetrate fully.
Complete Hollowing Workflow
  1. Import and orient the model correctly first
  2. Add supports (hollowing can shift geometry, so support after orientating but you can hollow before or after — just re-check supports)
  3. Hollow with appropriate wall thickness (2mm for most models)
  4. Set infill: None for display models, Grid for structural
  5. Add 2+ drain holes — lowest point and air vent at top
  6. X-ray check: Verify cavity, wall consistency, hole penetration
  7. Slice and check layer preview for any closed-off hollow sections
  8. Print — after washing, resin should drain freely from both holes
Post-print hollow care: After washing, shake the model to confirm no trapped liquid. Shine a UV flashlight through the drain holes to cure any residual resin inside the cavity before final curing.
Infill Options

None (Fully Hollow)

  • Maximum resin savings
  • Lightest result
  • Good for display models, figurines, busts
  • Wall thickness must be adequate (2mm+)

Grid / Triangle Lattice

  • Internal scaffolding structure
  • Adds rigidity to hollow shell
  • Uses more resin than hollow but less than solid
  • Good for larger structural parts or models that will be handled heavily
  • Note: makes drain holes less effective — ensure lattice cells connect to drain locations
Advanced

Orientation — The Most Overlooked Skill

Perfect settings can't save a badly oriented print. Master orientation and you'll solve most print failures before they happen.

Remember: In MSLA printing, the build plate is at the TOP. The print hangs INVERTED. What appears as the "bottom" in Chitubox is what prints first and adheres to the build plate. The "top" in Chitubox is actually the last thing that prints, hanging farthest from the plate.
Core Orientation Principles
  1. Tilt the model 15–45° to avoid large flat areas parallel to the FEP. This distributes the peel force across fewer pixels per layer rather than the entire face at once.
  2. Orient cosmetically important surfaces AWAY from where supports will attach. Supports leave scars — put those scars on the back, bottom, or hidden surfaces.
  3. No "suction cup" faces: Large flat surfaces parallel to the FEP create enormous vacuum forces during each layer peel. This tears supports and distorts the print. Tilt to break the seal.
  4. Long thin elements (arms, legs, horns): angle 45° to reduce the unsupported span length of each thin section.
  5. Critical features print more crisply in Z (vertical): Holes, threads, cylinders — orient so these features are aligned with the Z axis for cleanest results.
Common Orientation Mistakes
MistakeWhat Goes WrongFix
Flat on plate (0° tilt) Maximum suction force on every layer, severe elephant foot, complete loss of bottom-face detail, very difficult to remove from plate Tilt 30–45°. Use supports and raft instead of direct plate contact.
Perfectly vertical (90°) Maximum print height = maximum print time. Tallest Z = most potential for layer shift. Though good for some geometries (cylinders, columns). Tilt 15–30° off vertical for most organic shapes. Keep vertical for true cylindrical forms.
Cosmetic face toward FEP Face is the first thing printed — supports attach here. Face is covered in support scars. Ruined detail on the most important surface. Rotate 180°. Face should point away from the build plate (toward where you're looking in the viewport's "top view").
Hollow area opening facing down Resin pools inside hollow cavity. Drain holes don't drain effectively. Trapped resin causes post-cure cracking. Orient hollow opening / drain holes at true bottom of print orientation.
Optimal Orientations by Model Type
Model TypeRecommended OrientationReason
Humanoid figures 30–45° tilt on both X and Y axes, face pointing away from supports Distributes limb overhangs, protects facial detail
Rings / jewelry 45° angle, stone face up, band angled Stone surface protected from supports, band overhang manageable at angle
Miniatures (D&D scale) 45° tilt toward the back, face and front detail up and unsupported Preserves facial detail, weapon/shield details on front remain clean
Flat panels / signs 45° angle (NEVER flat on plate) Flat = maximum suction. 45° breaks suction, dramatically reduces support count.
Mechanical / functional parts Orient so hole axes are vertical (along Z) Circular holes print cleanest in Z direction — XY holes show pixel stepping
Cups / vessels / bowls Upside down (opening faces toward build plate, at top of slicer view) Avoids interior supports, eliminates suction trap, resin drains naturally during print
Busts Slight backward tilt (5–15°), facing slightly upward Nose and chin less likely to need supports, back of head takes supports instead
Orientation Tools in Chitubox
Auto-Orient Analyze menu → Auto Orientation. Algorithms suggest lowest-support orientation. Not always perfect but saves time as a starting point.
Manual Rotate Press E → drag axis rings OR click the axis field and type exact degree value (e.g., X: 45, Y: 15, Z: 0)
Lay Flat Select a face → "Lay Flat" button — snaps that face to be parallel to the build plate. Useful for getting a baseline orientation.

Orientation workflow tip: Try 3–4 different orientations with quick auto-support previews. Count the supports and note which surfaces they attach to. Pick the orientation with fewest supports on cosmetic surfaces — not necessarily fewest supports overall.
Understanding Suction Force

When a large flat surface is parallel to the FEP, the cured resin layer acts like a suction cup during the lift phase. The force required to peel it grows with the area of the flat surface.

  • A 50mm × 50mm flat base (2,500mm²) at 1 layer peel can require 5–15kg of force to separate from FEP
  • Your supports are only rated for a fraction of that — they snap
  • Or the FEP stretches, the layer deforms, and you get a warped, failed print

Tilting 45° reduces the effective flat area per layer to a thin diagonal strip — peel force drops by 80–90%.

Large flat-bottomed models (terrain, bases, flat signs) MUST be tilted. This is the #1 cause of mysterious "everything seemed right but it failed" prints.
Orientation Quick Reference

Do This

  • Tilt most models 30–45°
  • Protect cosmetic faces from supports
  • Orient holes along Z axis
  • Point cup/bowl openings down (toward plate = up in slicer)
  • Angle thin protrusions to minimize span

Avoid This

  • Flat on plate (no tilt)
  • Cosmetic face toward FEP
  • Large flat areas parallel to FEP
  • Hollow openings facing away from drain path
  • Long overhangs without 45° angle reduction

Resin Types & Material Guide

Standard Resin Types

ABS-Like Resin

Most common type. Tough, slightly flexible, excellent detail. Brands: Elegoo ABS-Like, Anycubic Standard. Ideal temp: 20–28°C.

Water-Washable Resin

Clean up with water instead of IPA. Convenient but slightly more brittle and requires slower exposure. Brand: Elegoo Water Washable.

Plant-Based / Eco Resin

Reduced VOCs, water cleanup variants available. Brands: Phrozen Aqua, Elegoo Plant-Based.

Tough / Engineering Resin

Impact resistant with ABS-like mechanical properties. Brands: Siraya Tech Blu, Elegoo ABS-Like Pro.

Flexible / Rubber-Like

Resin Mixing Tips

  • Always shake or stir the resin bottle before pouring — pigment settles over hours.
  • Gently stir resin in the tank with a silicone spatula before every print, especially after sitting overnight.
  • Temperature matters: below 20°C causes slower cure and more failures. Use a resin heater or warm room.
  • Mixing resins: Tenacious (flexible) + ABS-Like = tougher blend. Start at 10% Tenacious, increase for more flex.
  • Old resin: check for cloudiness or gel chunks. Strain through a paint strainer before use.

Resin Storage & Disposal

  • Store in the original opaque bottle, away from sunlight and UV sources.
  • Shelf life: 12 months sealed, 3–6 months opened.
  • Never pour used resin back without straining — it may contain cured particles.
  • Disposal: Cure fully (sunlight or UV lamp) then dispose as solid waste. Never pour liquid resin down the drain.

Wash & Cure — Post-Processing Complete

Golden Rule: Never touch uncured resin with bare skin. Always wear gloves until UV curing is fully complete.

Washing

Purpose: remove uncured liquid resin from the print surface.

Method 1 — IPA (Isopropyl Alcohol 95%+)

  • Two-bucket method: Bucket 1 = dirty IPA (rough wash, agitate 1–2 min). Bucket 2 = clean IPA (final rinse, 30s–1 min).
  • Or use a dedicated wash station: Elegoo Mercury Plus, Anycubic Wash & Cure.
  • Time: 2–3 minutes total. Over-washing causes white frost and weakened parts (IPA swells resin).
  • Water-washable resin: warm water, 2–3 min agitation. No IPA needed (though IPA also works).
  • After wash: blow off excess liquid, let dry fully before curing.

Method 2 — Dedicated Resin Cleaner

  • Options: Resinaway, Mean Green (water-based, safer than IPA).
  • Less flammable, longer usable life than IPA.
Drying before cure: 5–10 minutes air dry, or use a hair dryer on cool setting. Do NOT cure while wet — IPA interferes with surface cure, causing a white chalky surface.

Curing

Purpose: complete the UV crosslinking reaction to achieve full hardness and mechanical properties.

  • UV wavelength: 405nm (standard for most MSLA resins).
  • Curing time: 2–6 min per side for small prints; 6–10 min for large pieces.
  • Cure in water: submerging during cure improves surface quality — water excludes oxygen which inhibits cure.
  • Temperature: warm curing (~40°C) improves final hardness. Some stations include a warming function.
  • Over-curing: causes yellowing and brittleness. Find the minimum time for full hardness.
  • Under-curing: sticky surface, weaker properties. If still tacky after full time, increase cure duration or check UV lamp power.
Cure both sides: rotate print halfway through cure time to ensure all surfaces receive UV exposure.

Elegoo Mercury Plus Workflow

Support Removal

  • Remove supports before final cure = easier (print remains slightly flexible).
  • Or remove after curing: use flush cutters, dental picks, needle-nose pliers.
  • Support scars: sand with 400 → 800 → 2000 grit wet sandpaper for a smooth surface.
  • Cleanup tools: dental picks, flush cutters, craft knife, fine files.

Troubleshooting — Every Resin Problem

Print Failure (detaches from plate, stays on FEP, or falls)

CauseFix
Z offset wrong (plate not close enough to FEP)Re-level and reset Z=0
Bottom exposure too shortIncrease bottom exposure by 10s at a time
Plate surface contaminatedClean with IPA, lightly sand with 400 grit
Supports too light or thinUse medium or heavy supports, add more contact points
Large suction-cup surface on modelRe-orient and tilt 45°
FEP damaged or cloudyReplace FEP film

Layer Separation

Cause: exposure too low, lift speed too high (tearing layers before cure completes).

Fix: increase normal exposure by 0.5s, reduce lift speed to 2mm/s, reduce retract speed.

Elephant Foot (first layers wider than model)

Cause: bottom exposure too long, Z offset too close, no elephant foot compensation active.

Fix: reduce bottom exposure by 10s, set elephant foot compensation +0.1mm in Chitubox.

Support Failures

Cause: supports too thin, contact point too small.

Fix: change to medium/heavy supports, increase contact tip diameter to 0.4mm or larger.

Suction Cupping (loud crack, FEP distortion)

Cause: sealed hollow area or flat surface creating vacuum during peel.

Fix: add drain holes to hollow models, tilt flat surfaces 45°, add vent holes.

Cloudy / Frosty Surface

CauseFix
Over-washed in IPAReduce wash time
IPA contaminated (>30% resin saturation)Replace IPA
Cured while still wet with IPADry completely before curing

Sticky Surface After Curing

CauseFix
Under-curedExtend cure time or check UV lamp power
Oxygen inhibitionCure submerged in water for better surface result
Resin formula quirkTry longer cure time — some resins need 10+ minutes

Print Sticking to FEP (won't release during print)

Cause: FEP too tight or cloudy, lift speed too slow, resin too viscous (cold room).

Fix: replace FEP if cloudy, increase lift speed slightly, warm resin to 25–30°C, consider FEP release spray.

Z-Banding / Visible Layer Lines

Cause: Z axis lead screw binding, under-lubrication, or vibration.

Fix: lubricate Z screw with PTFE grease, tighten Z rail eccentric nuts, reduce lift/retract speed.

Resin Smell / Fumes

  • Use an activated carbon air purifier or place printer in a ventilated enclosure.
  • Elegoo Mercury enclosure includes a built-in activated carbon filter.
  • Keep the room ventilated during and after printing sessions.

FEP Replacement Guide

  • When to replace: FEP appears cloudy or scratched, or print success rate drops despite correct settings.
  • How often: every 3–6 months with regular use, or approximately every 1–2L of resin printed.
  • Tightening: tighten evenly by alternating corners — like tightening lug nuts on a car wheel.

Expert Tips & Resin Mastery

Workflow Speed Tips

  • Pre-arrange: set up multiple prints on one plate — overlapping support structures is acceptable, overlapping models is not.
  • Save .chitubox project files — reopens with all settings, orientation, and supports intact.
  • Printer profiles: set up and save dedicated profiles per printer + resin combination.
  • Batch profiles: different profiles for miniatures vs display vs functional parts.
  • Queue overnight: load multiple files and slice them while you sleep.

Resin Maintenance

  • After every session: pour resin back through a strainer into the bottle. Don't leave resin in the tank more than 3–5 days (cloudy resin = failed prints).
  • FEP care: never use a metal spatula on FEP. Use only silicone or soft plastic tools.
  • Tank cleaning: pour out resin, wipe with IPA, cure any remaining resin with a UV torch and peel it off.
  • Build plate cleaning: IPA wipe before every print. Ensure no old resin chunks are stuck. Always remove failed prints from FEP before starting a new print.
  • Quarterly: check all screws, check Z rail for play, lubricate the Z lead screw.

Environmental Tips

  • Temperature: keep resin at 20–25°C. Cold environments cause failures. Use a small space heater near the printer in winter.
  • Humidity: high humidity slightly affects cure times, but is not critical (unlike FDM moisture sensitivity).
  • UV from windows: close blinds during printing. Sunlight partially cures resin in the tank.
  • Resin odor: use an activated carbon filter in enclosed spaces. Elegoo makes printer enclosures with built-in filtration.
