Understanding Fusion 360

Fusion 360 is Autodesk's cloud-connected, all-in-one parametric CAD/CAM/CAE platform. It combines mechanical design, product engineering, manufacturing preparation, and simulation in a single application — accessible on Windows and macOS with cloud storage built in.

🔷 What Is Fusion 360?

Fusion 360 is used by mechanical engineers, product designers, makers, and manufacturers worldwide. Unlike traditional CAD tools, it runs designs in the cloud (Autodesk cloud), allows version history, and integrates CAD → CAM → Simulation in one window — so you can design a part and generate CNC toolpaths without leaving the app.

  • Parametric CAD — every dimension is a living variable, not a fixed number
  • CAM (Computer-Aided Manufacturing) — generate CNC, lathe, and additive toolpaths
  • CAE / Simulation — finite element analysis (FEA) for stress, thermal, modal
  • Rendering — photorealistic product renders with cloud or local GPU
  • Drawing — 2D engineering drawings (title blocks, views, tolerances)

🧠 The Parametric Philosophy

Fusion 360 is built on parametric modeling — a fundamentally different approach from "dumb" solid modelers or mesh editors:

  • Every dimension is editable — change a sketch dimension and the entire model updates automatically
  • The Timeline records history — operations are stored in sequence; you can go back and edit any past step
  • Constraints drive geometry — lines aren't just lines; they have relationships (parallel, perpendicular, tangent) that are maintained when you edit
  • Intent is preserved — if you specify a hole is centered on a face, it stays centered even when the face resizes
Why This MattersIn a non-parametric tool, changing a dimension means redrawing. In Fusion 360, changing "width=50mm" to "width=80mm" updates every sketch, extrude, hole, and pattern that references it — instantly.
🔑 8 Key Concepts Every Beginner Must Learn
  • 1
    Sketch → 3D Feature Workflow — Always sketch first (a 2D profile), then apply a 3D feature (Extrude, Revolve, Loft). You cannot extrude without a sketch profile. This is the fundamental loop of parametric CAD.
  • 2
    Fully Constrained Sketches — Blue lines are unconstrained (free to move), Black lines are fully constrained (locked — this is your goal), Red lines are over-constrained or conflicting (fix immediately). Always finish a sketch fully black before extruding.
  • 3
    The Timeline (bottom of screen) — Every sketch, extrude, fillet, and joint is a step in the timeline. Right-click any step to edit, suppress, delete, or move it. The timeline is what makes Fusion 360 parametric.
  • 4
    Parameters — User-defined variables like width=50mm or wallThick=3mm. These are the power user's superpower. Instead of typing "50" in a dimension, type "width" — now every feature that uses "width" updates together when you change the parameter.
  • 5
    Bodies vs Components — A Body is a piece of geometry (a shape). A Component is a "part" with its own origin, coordinate system, and parameters — used in assemblies. Rule: use Components whenever you're building an assembly.
  • 6
    Origin Planes (XY / YZ / XZ) — Every model starts relative to three infinite reference planes. Always start your first sketch on an origin plane. This grounds your model in space and makes future operations predictable.
  • 7
    Capture Design History vs Direct Modeling — Fusion has two modes. Capture Design History ON (default): parametric, timeline visible, fully editable. Direct Modeling: push/pull faces freely, no history — faster for quick edits but loses parametric ability.
  • 8
    Saving — Ctrl+S saves to Autodesk cloud (version history). File → Export saves locally as .f3d, .step, .iges, .stl, etc. Always use Ctrl+S frequently during work.

Sketching — The Foundation of Everything

In Fusion 360, almost every 3D feature starts with a 2D sketch. Master sketching — constraints, dimensions, and the color-code system — and the rest of Fusion 360 falls into place naturally.

▶ Creating a Sketch
  1. Go to Solid tab (or Surface/Sheet Metal) → Create Sketch
  2. Click a plane (XY, YZ, or XZ origin plane) or a flat face on an existing solid
  3. The view snaps to look at the sketch plane. You're now in Sketch mode.
  4. Draw geometry, add constraints and dimensions, then click Stop Sketch or press Escape → Finish Sketch
TipYou can also right-click on any flat face in the canvas and choose Create Sketch — Fusion attaches the sketch directly to that face.
🎨 Sketch Color Codes — CRITICAL — Memorize These

The color of sketch geometry tells you its constraint state at a glance:

Blue Unconstrained — the element can still move or resize freely. Needs more constraints or dimensions.
Black Fully constrained — position and size are completely locked. This is the goal. Every sketch should be all-black before extruding.
Red Over-constrained or conflicting — too many conflicting constraints applied. Fix immediately by removing a constraint or dimension.
Yellow Reference/Construction geometry — guide lines for sketching. Not used as profiles for 3D features. Toggle construction mode with X key.
Red = Fix ImmediatelyA red sketch usually means you applied two constraints that contradict each other (e.g., a line that's both Horizontal AND Vertical). Check the Sketch Palette and remove the conflicting constraint.
Why Fully Constrained?A blue (unconstrained) sketch can be accidentally dragged or changed later in the timeline. Fully black sketches are immune to accidental edits and communicate clear design intent.

🔒 All Sketch Constraints — Complete Reference

Constraints define relationships between sketch elements. They reduce degrees of freedom without specifying a numeric value. To apply: click the constraint icon in the toolbar, then click the geometry — or right-click geometry for the constraint list.

  • ◎
    Coincident — two points share the exact same location. Most common constraint — connects endpoints of lines.
  • —
    Collinear — two separate lines lie on the same infinite line (they're colinear even if not touching).
  • ⊙
    Concentric — two circles, arcs, or ellipses share the same center point.
  • ⊥
    Midpoint — a point is constrained to lie exactly at the midpoint of a line or arc.
  • ⊕
    Fixed / Ground — the element is locked in absolute position and cannot move at all. Use sparingly — usually prefer dimensioning from origin instead.
  • ∥
    Parallel — two lines are parallel to each other (same direction, any distance apart).
  • ⊥
    Perpendicular — two lines meet at exactly 90°. They don't have to intersect — the angle between their directions is 90°.
  • ↔
    Horizontal — a line is constrained to be exactly horizontal (parallel to X-axis). Also works on points: forces them to the same Y value.
  • ↕
    Vertical — a line is constrained to be exactly vertical (parallel to Y-axis). Also forces two points to the same X value.
  • ⌒
    Tangent — a line, arc, or spline meets a circle or arc smoothly with no sharp corner. Critical for blending curves.
  • ~
    Smooth (G2) — a spline meets an adjacent curve with curvature continuity (not just tangency). The rate of curvature change is also matched — produces the smoothest possible blends.
  • ⟺
    Symmetric — two elements are mirror images of each other across a centerline. Edit one side and the other mirrors automatically.
  • =
    Equal — two lines have the same length, or two circles/arcs have the same radius. Change one and the other follows.

Driving vs Driven Dimensions

  • Driving dimension — controls the sketch geometry. Change the value, the geometry moves.
  • Driven dimension (reference) — shown in parentheses, e.g. (45.00). Tells you a measurement but doesn't control it (it's already fully constrained by other means).

Parametric Dimensions — The Key Technique

When the dimension dialog opens, instead of typing a number, type a name=value pair like width=50 mm or just reference an existing parameter like wallThick. Fusion creates a User Parameter automatically and links the dimension to it.

Power MoveType width/2 or height*0.75 in a dimension box — Fusion evaluates expressions. This lets you drive related dimensions mathematically without creating separate parameters for everything.
⚙️ Parameters Deep Dive

Access the Parameters dialog via Modify → Parameters (or press P while in a sketch — note: P in canvas = Project, but in the menu = Parameters).

User Parameters

  • Created by you: Height = 100 mm
  • WallThick = 3 mm
  • BoltDiam = 6 mm
  • Referenced by name in any dimension
  • Change one → all linked dims update

Model Parameters

  • Auto-created by Fusion for each dim
  • Named d1, d2, d3… by default
  • Can be renamed and reused
  • Visible in Parameters dialog

Supported Expressions in Parameters

Best PracticeBefore sketching anything, open Parameters and define your key dimensions: overall Width, Height, Depth, WallThick, BoltDiam, etc. Then reference these by name as you sketch. This turns your model into a reusable template — change one number and everything updates.
✅ Sketching Workflow Best Practices
  1. Set Parameters first — define key dimensions in Modify → Parameters before starting
  2. Start from the origin — lock your first point or line to the origin (Coincident with the origin point)
  3. Sketch the shape, then constrain — rough shape first, then add geometric constraints, then add dimensions
  4. Constrain before dimensioning — parallel/perpendicular/tangent constraints should be applied before numeric dimensions
  5. Use construction lines — press X to toggle construction mode for centerlines and guide lines
  6. Stop only when all-black — don't proceed to 3D features until the sketch is fully constrained (all black lines)
  7. Name your sketches — double-click the sketch in the browser and rename it (e.g., "Profile - Side Bracket") for clarity
Common MistakeExtruding a blue (partially constrained) sketch. The sketch looks fine but can be accidentally modified later, causing the extruded feature to change unexpectedly. Always get to all-black first.

3D Features — Complete Reference

These are the tools that turn your 2D sketches into 3D solid geometry. Every feature in this list is parametric — edit the inputs and the geometry updates. Accessed from the Solid tab in the Design workspace.

📦 EXTRUDE E

The most-used 3D feature. Takes a closed 2D sketch profile and pushes it in a direction to create a solid.

Direction Options

  • One Side — extrude in one direction only
  • Symmetric — equal distance both sides of the sketch plane
  • Two Sides — different distances on each side independently

Extent Options

  • Distance — exact numeric distance
  • To Object — extrude until it hits a selected face or body
  • To Body — extrude until it fills the boundary of another body
  • Through All — infinite — goes through everything (great for cut operations)
  • To Next — stops at the very next face it encounters

Operation (what to do with the result)

  • Join — merge extruded volume with existing solid (Boolean union)
  • Cut — subtract extruded volume from existing solid (Boolean difference)
  • Intersect — keep only the overlapping volume
  • New Body — create an independent body (not merged)
  • New Component — create as a separate component

Additional Options

  • Taper Angle — add draft (inward or outward taper) as you extrude
  • Chain Faces — automatically include adjacent tangent faces
Quick TipClick any flat face on the canvas and press E — Fusion opens Extrude with that face pre-selected. Drag the blue arrow to extrude interactively.
🔄 REVOLVE

Rotates a sketch profile around an axis line to create a solid of revolution (axially symmetric parts: shafts, bottles, bowls, discs).

  • Select the Profile (closed sketch region) and the Axis (a sketch line, edge, or axis)
  • Angle options: full 360°, partial angle (e.g., 180° for a half-shell), or symmetric (±angle)
  • Operations: Join / Cut / Intersect / New Body (same as Extrude)
  • A centerline in the sketch serves as the revolve axis automatically if it's a construction line
Best forTurned parts (lathe parts): shafts, flanges, pulleys, knobs, bottles, lenses. The sketch should be on one side of the axis — the profile represents the cross-section that gets rotated.
🌊 SWEEP

Extrudes a profile along a curved path instead of a straight line. Creates tubes, rails, custom extrusions, and any shape that follows a curve.

  • Profile — the cross-section shape (must be perpendicular to path start)
  • Path — the curve the profile travels along (can be open or closed)
  • Guide Rail — additional curve that controls how the profile scales or rotates as it sweeps
  • Orientation — Perpendicular to Path: profile always stays perpendicular to path direction (normal sweep)
  • Orientation — Parallel to Original: profile keeps its original orientation throughout (parallel translation)
  • Twist Angle: rotate the profile as it travels along the path
Example UsesPipe/tube bends, door handles, guitar bodies, cable channels, helical springs (use a helix path).
🏔️ LOFT

Creates a smooth solid by blending between two or more profiles at different positions. The profiles can be different shapes — circles, rectangles, splines, or anything.

  • Add profiles in order (each profile is a cross-section at a different position)
  • Rails — optional guide curves that control the shape of the blend between profiles
  • Centerline Loft — all profiles lie along a centerline curve
  • Profile Connections — "Matched" (smooth transition) or "Tangent" (tangent to adjacent geometry)
  • Operations: Join / Cut / Intersect / New Body
Example UsesAirplane fuselages, car hoods, bottle necks, nozzle transitions (circle to rectangle), ergonomic grip shapes.
⭕ HOLE

Dedicated hole tool — smarter than just cutting a revolved circle because it understands engineering intent (countersinks, threads, standard sizes).

Hole Types

  • Simple — straight cylindrical hole
  • Counterbore (↲) — wider recessed area at top for bolt head to sit flush
  • Countersink (∨) — conical recess for flat-head screws
  • Spotface — shallow flat area around the hole for a washer face

Drill Tip

  • Flat (end mills)
  • 118° (standard twist drill)
  • Custom angle

Depth Options

  • Blind — goes to a specific depth
  • Through All — goes completely through
  • To Object — stops at a specified face

Thread Option

  • Enable threading inside the hole
  • Choose thread type (M metric, UNC, UNF, etc.)
  • Thread class (1A, 2A, 3A for external; 1B, 2B, 3B for internal)
  • Cosmetic (fast, display only) or Modeled (actual geometry)
PlacementPlace a sketch Point where you want the hole center, then use the Hole tool. Or click the Hole tool and click directly on a face — Fusion lets you snap to edges and centers for placement.
🔩 THREAD

Applies thread specification to a cylindrical face (external shaft or internal hole). Covers all standard thread types.

  • Select a cylindrical face (shaft exterior or hole interior)
  • Thread Types: M (ISO metric), UNC (Unified Coarse), UNF (Unified Fine), BSPP, NPT, and many more
  • Cosmetic Thread — fast display-only representation, shown as a surface feature, no geometry added to model. Recommended for assemblies (faster)
  • Modeled Thread — actual helical groove cut into/onto the cylinder. Accurate geometry for renders and FEA. Slower, heavier file.
  • Thread Class — fit tolerance (2A = standard external, 2B = standard internal)
  • Full Length — thread runs the entire length of the cylinder, or specify a depth
🟡 FILLET F

Rounds sharp edges. Essential for safety, aesthetics, and reducing stress concentrations in engineering parts.

  • Select edges → set radius → preview → OK
  • Tangent Chain — automatically select all edges tangent to the clicked edge
  • Feature Set — select all edges created by a specific feature
  • G1 (Tangent) — standard fillet, tangent to adjacent faces
  • G2 (Curvature Continuous) — smoother blend, curvature also matches adjacent faces — used in premium product design
  • Variable Radius Fillet — set a different radius at each endpoint of an edge. Useful for tapered transitions.
  • Full Round Fillet — selects three faces; removes the middle face and replaces it entirely with a tangent round blend
Order MattersApply fillets last, after all cuts, holes, and features. Adding a fillet early and then cutting across it often causes errors. Exception: functional fillets (like sealing radiuses) that define the geometry.
◤ CHAMFER

Creates a beveled (angled flat) edge instead of a round. Common in machined parts, entry chamfers on holes, and decorative edges.

  • Equal Distance — same distance on both faces (45° chamfer)
  • Distance + Angle — specify one distance and the angle of the bevel
  • Two Distances — different distances on each adjacent face
  • Select individual edges or use tangent chain / feature set selection
🫙 SHELL

Hollows out a solid body to create a thin-walled part. Essential for plastic enclosures, containers, and any hollow product.

  • Select one or more faces to remove (these become the opening — like removing the lid)
  • Set the wall thickness
  • Inside — shell removes material inward (external dimensions preserved)
  • Outside — shell adds material outward (internal dimensions preserved)
  • Both — adds material equally in both directions
  • Multiple Thickness — assign different thicknesses to different faces (e.g., thicker bottom, thinner walls)
ExampleDesign a solid box → Shell it with the top face removed → you have a hollow container with walls and a bottom of specified thickness. Then add a lip, threads, or a lid component.
📐 DRAFT

Applies a taper angle to faces. Required for injection-molded parts to allow the part to release from the mold without sticking.

  • Fixed Plane method — specify the pull direction (mold open direction) and apply draft to perpendicular faces
  • Pull Direction method — define the direction the mold opens and apply draft automatically
  • Parting Line method — draft above and below a parting line (for two-piece molds)
  • Typical draft angles: 1°–3° for plastic, up to 7° for rough textures
Manufacturing RequirementIf you're designing for injection molding or die casting, always add draft before finalizing. No draft = part sticks in mold = expensive tooling problems.
🪞 MIRROR (Solid)

Creates a mirrored copy of features or bodies across a plane. Far faster than recreating geometry on both sides.

  • Select Features (parametric steps in the timeline) or Bodies
  • Select a Mirror Plane (origin plane, flat face, or construction plane)
  • Mirrored features are linked by default — edit the original and the mirror updates
  • To make an independent copy: Combine → New Body and pattern it separately
⬛⬛⬛ PATTERN

Rectangular Pattern

  • Direction 1 + Direction 2
  • Distance between instances (or spacing total)
  • Quantity in each direction
  • Fill area option: fill a region automatically

Circular Pattern

  • Select axis of rotation
  • Angle range (full 360° or partial)
  • Quantity of instances
  • Symmetric option

Pattern on Path

  • Follow a curve (open or closed)
  • Evenly distributed or manual spacing
  • Orient to path (rotate with curve direction) or keep orientation fixed

What to Pattern

  • Features (specific timeline steps)
  • Bodies (entire solid bodies)
  • Faces (specific faces only)
  • Components (in assemblies)

Adds material thickness to a surface (2D surface body) to convert it into a solid 3D body. Used after surface modeling to create shell-like structures.

  • Set thickness amount and direction (one side / symmetric / both sides)
  • Operations: New Body / Join / Cut / Intersect

🌊 SURFACE MODELING (Patch Workspace / Surface Tab)

When to Use Surfaces

Use surface modeling when solid features can't achieve the required shape — complex aerodynamic curves, automotive body panels, ergonomic organic housings, or any shape where you need precise control over curvature.

Patch

  • Fill an open boundary (a set of connected edges or curves) with a surface
  • Set boundary continuity: None (sharp), Tangent (G1), or Curvature (G2) at each edge
  • Add internal rails for more shape control

Loft (Surface)

  • Same as solid loft but creates a surface skin between profiles
  • Add guide rails for controlled shape between profiles
  • Used for smooth aerodynamic skins: car hoods, aircraft panels

Ruled Surface

  • Extends a surface outward from an existing edge at a controllable angle
  • Used to create tangent or normal extensions for trimming operations

Offset Surface

  • Creates a parallel copy of a surface at a specified distance
  • Useful for creating parting surfaces or clearance surfaces

Trim / Extend / Merge

  • Trim: cut a surface using another surface or curve as the cutting tool
  • Extend: lengthen a surface edge by a distance, tangent or curvature continuous
  • Merge: combine multiple adjacent surfaces into one unified surface body

Surface Modeling Workflow

  1. Sketch cross-section profiles and guide rail curves in 3D space
  2. Create Loft surfaces between profiles using rails for shape control
  3. Use Patch to close any open boundaries
  4. Trim surfaces against each other to create clean intersections
  5. Merge all surfaces into a closed, watertight surface body
  6. Use Thicken to convert the surface body to a solid
  7. Add fillets and other solid features to complete the model
Watertight CheckBefore Thicken, ensure your surface body is completely closed (no gaps or holes). Use Inspect → Surface Analysis to check curvature distribution and find problem areas.
⚙️ PARAMETERS — Advanced Mastery

Building a Fully Parametric Model — The Process

  1. Open Modify → Parameters before starting
  2. Create all key User Parameters: Width, Height, Depth, WallThick, BoltDiam, Clearance, etc.
  3. Sketch using parameter names instead of numbers everywhere
  4. When a dimension appears that derives from others, use an expression: Width/2, Height - WallThick*2
  5. Test: change one master parameter and verify the entire model updates correctly

Linking Parameters (Dependencies)

  • WallThick → drives Shell Thickness
  • Height → drives multiple feature depths
  • BoltDiam → drives Hole diameter + Counterbore
  • BoltDiam * 1.5 → automatically sized counterbore

Supported Math Functions

  • sqrt(x) — square root
  • sin(x deg), cos(x deg), tan(x deg)
  • abs(x) — absolute value
  • max(a, b), min(a, b)
  • floor(x), ceil(x), round(x)
  • PI — π constant

Conditional Parameters (Limited Support)

Fusion 360 has limited if() support in parameters. For complex conditional logic, consider using design variants or configurations instead. Simple example: use maximum/minimum clamps with max(MinWall, ComputedWall).

Exporting & Importing Parameter Tables

In the Parameters dialog, use the Export CSV button to save your parameter table. Share it with team members or import it into another Fusion file via Import CSV. This is powerful for standardized part families.

🔗 The 7 Joint Types — Complete Reference

Each joint type constrains specific degrees of freedom (DOF). A rigid body in free space has 6 DOF (3 translation, 3 rotation). Each joint type removes some of those freedoms.

1

Rigid 0 DOF

Parts are completely locked together — zero degrees of freedom. No relative motion at all. Like two parts that are glued, welded, or bolted solid. Use for sub-assemblies that act as one unit.

2

Revolute 1 DOF

Rotates around a single axis. The hinge joint — like a door hinge, pivot pin, motor shaft bearing, or any pin connection. Can set angle limits (min/max rotation angle). Most common joint type in mechanisms.

3

Slider 1 DOF

Translates (slides) along a single axis. Like a drawer, piston, linear rail, or telescoping tube. Can set position limits (min/max travel distance). No rotation — pure linear motion.

4

Cylindrical 2 DOF

Rotation AND translation on the same axis simultaneously — like a nut on a bolt that can both spin freely and slide along the bolt. Or a shaft in a bearing that can both rotate and slide axially.

5

Pin-Slot 2 DOF

Rotation around one axis PLUS translation along a different axis. Like a pin riding in a slot — it pivots and slides in the slot simultaneously. Used in cam mechanisms and Geneva drives.

6

Planar 3 DOF

Translation in a plane (X and Y) PLUS rotation around the perpendicular axis (Z). Like a puck sliding freely on an air-hockey table. 3 degrees of freedom total. Used for parts that can move and rotate within a flat surface.

7

Ball 3 DOF rotation

Rotation in all 3 directions — like a ball-and-socket joint (your shoulder or hip joint). Cannot translate — the center point is fixed. Used for universal joints, ball joints, and articulated connections. Can set cone angle limit.

➕ Adding Joints — Step by Step

Standard Joint

  1. Go to Assemble → Joint (or press J)
  2. Click the Joint Origin on the first component (snap to vertex, center, midpoint, or face)
  3. Click the Joint Origin on the second component
  4. Fusion moves the components together at those points
  5. Select the Joint Type from the dropdown
  6. Set the Motion axis if needed (which direction does it rotate/slide)
  7. Set Limits (min angle, max angle, or min/max position) if applicable
  8. Click OK

As-Built Joint

Assemble → As-Built Joint — adds a joint without moving the components. Fusion defines the joint at their current position. Use this when you've already positioned components where you want them (via Move/Copy) and just need to add motion constraints.

Joint Origins

Joint Origins are the connection points. They snap to: vertices, edge midpoints, face centers, arc/circle centers, or you can place them at a custom location. For a hinge: snap to the center of the hinge pin hole on each component — Fusion lines them up.

Grounding the BaseBefore adding joints, right-click your base/fixed component in the browser → Ground. A grounded component is locked in space. This gives joints a stable reference and prevents the whole assembly from floating when you add motion.
🎬 MOTION STUDY

Create an animation that shows how your mechanism moves. Access via Assemble → Motion Study.

  • Set the motion for each joint: angle or position over time (key frames)
  • Add multiple key frames to create complex multi-step animations
  • Play the animation in real time in the canvas

💥 CONTACT SETS — Collision Detection

By default, components in Fusion 360 can pass through each other during motion simulation. Contact Sets prevent this.

  • Enable via: Assemble → Enable Contact Sets
  • Select which component pairs should physically interact (collide)
  • During motion simulation, the selected components stop when they touch
  • Useful for checking: cam follower motion, drawer stop limits, gear tooth contact
Performance NoteContact computation is expensive. Only enable Contact Sets for the specific pairs you need to check — not for every component in a large assembly.
🔍 INTERFERENCE CHECK

Static check for overlapping (intersecting) volumes between components — a critical quality step before manufacturing.

  • Access via: Inspect → Interference
  • Select the components or bodies to check against each other
  • Fusion highlights overlapping volumes in red and reports their volume
  • Zero interference = components fit correctly in 3D space
  • Any interference = you have a design error — components physically overlap, which is impossible in reality
  • Run this check before sending to manufacturing
Always Run Before ManufacturingInterference check catches clearance errors that are impossible to see visually in a complex assembly. A 0.1mm overlap between a shaft and a housing means the assembly won't go together — find it in the model before machining the parts.
⚙️ MOTION LINK — Coupled Motion

Link two joints so their motion is mathematically coupled — one moves, the other moves proportionally.

  • Access via: Assemble → Motion Link
  • Select two joints and define the ratio between their motions
  • Example — Gear pair: Revolute joint A (driving gear) linked to Revolute joint B (driven gear) with ratio 2:1. Turn gear A one revolution → gear B turns two revolutions.
  • Example — Rack and pinion: Revolute joint (pinion rotation) linked to Slider joint (rack translation). Pinion turns → rack translates proportionally.
  • Direction (same or reverse) can be set for the linked motion
✅ Assembly Best Practices
  • Plan your assembly structure first — top-level assembly → sub-assemblies → individual parts. Sketch a tree diagram before modeling.
  • Name every component immediately after creating it. "Component1", "Component2" becomes unmanageable fast.
  • Ground the base component — the frame, housing, or chassis that everything else attaches to. Right-click → Ground.
  • Add joints one at a time and test motion after each one. Don't add all joints at once then wonder why nothing works.
  • Use sub-assemblies for groups of parts that move together (e.g., a door with its hinges and handle is one sub-assembly).
  • Save components as individual files if they'll be reused across projects. Insert them via Insert → Insert Into Current Design.
  • Check interference at each major design stage — not just at the end.
  • Use the Browser visibility (lightbulb) aggressively — hide components to work on adjacent ones clearly.

Simulation & Rendering

Simulation (FEA — Finite Element Analysis)

What it is: Simulation lets you test how your design handles real-world forces, heat, and vibration before building a physical prototype — saving time, money, and material.

Setting Up a Static Stress Study

Switch to the Simulation workspace via the workspace dropdown.

  1. New Study → Static Stress
  2. Assign Material: right-click the body → Assign Material → pick from the library (Steel, Aluminum 6061-T6, Nylon, ABS, etc.)
  3. Apply Constraint (Fixed): Constraints → Fixed → select the faces that are bolted or grounded in real use
  4. Apply Load (Force): Loads → Force → select a face → set direction and magnitude (e.g. 1000 N downward)
  5. Solve: Solve → Solve (Fusion meshes the part and runs the FEA calculation — may take 1–5 min)
  6. View Results: Color map overlay appears — red = high stress, blue = low stress

Reading Simulation Results

Von Mises Stress

Combined equivalent stress value. If it exceeds the material's Yield Strength, the part will deform permanently.

Factor of Safety (FoS)

= Yield Strength ÷ Max Stress. Aim for 2.0+ for safety-critical parts, 1.5+ for non-critical. Values below 1.0 (red) = will fail.

Displacement Plot

Shows how much the part deflects under the applied load. Look for unexpected bending or deformation far from the load point.

Reaction Forces

Verify the sum of reaction forces at constraints equals your applied loads. Mismatch = incorrect boundary conditions.

Thermal Simulation

  1. New Study → Thermal
  2. Apply Temperature: fixed temperature on a face (simulating a heat source, e.g. 150°C)
  3. Apply Convection: air cooling — set convection coefficient (W/m²·K) and ambient temperature
  4. Solve → Results show temperature distribution across the part

Useful for: heatsinks, electronic enclosures, engine components, plastic parts near heat sources.

Modal Frequency Analysis

Finds the natural resonant frequencies of your part — the frequencies at which it will vibrate if excited.

  • New Study → Modal Frequencies
  • Apply fixed constraints (as in static stress), but no force loads
  • Solve → results show mode shapes and frequencies (Hz)
  • Critical rule: ensure operating frequency ≠ any resonant frequency — resonance causes rapid failure

Simulation Best Practices

Simplify geometry first: Remove small fillets, tiny holes, and cosmetic features that don't affect structural behavior. Simpler geometry = faster, more stable mesh.
  • Mesh refinement: Mesh Settings → reduce element size near stress concentrations (corners, holes, transitions) for more accurate results
  • Mesh independence study: keep refining the mesh until results stop changing significantly — that's your converged answer
  • Verify boundary conditions: wrong constraint placement is the #1 cause of wrong simulation results
  • Always check units: confirm forces are in Newtons, not pounds-force, and dimensions are consistent

CAM — Computer-Aided Manufacturing

What CAM does: Generates G-code — the machine instruction language that tells a CNC mill, router, or lathe exactly where to move its cutting tool, at what speed, and at what depth. Switch to the Manufacturing workspace to access CAM.

Core CAM Concepts

Stock

The raw material block you'll be cutting from — slightly larger than the finished part.

Tool

End mill, drill bit, ball nose, face mill — each has defined geometry (diameter, flute count, corner radius) that affects cutting behavior.

Toolpath

The precise 3D path the cutter follows through the material to achieve the desired shape.

Feed Rate

Speed the tool moves through material (mm/min or in/min). Too fast = tool breakage; too slow = inefficiency.

Spindle Speed

RPM of the rotating tool. Depends on material, tool diameter, and coating.

Depth of Cut (DOC)

How deep each pass cuts into the material. Axial DOC (Z depth) and radial DOC (side engagement).

Stepover

How far the tool shifts sideways between adjacent passes. Smaller stepover = smoother surface, more passes.

Setting Up a CAM Job

  1. Manufacturing → Setup → New Setup
  2. Set orientation: pick which face is the "top" of the part relative to the machine (Z+ is typically up)
  3. Set Stock: Bounding Box with a small positive offset (e.g. 2mm) — represents the raw material slightly larger than the part
  4. Create toolpath operations from the Manufacturing menu (2D, 3D, Drilling, etc.)

2D Operations (Most Common for Beginners)

2D Pocket — Clears out a recessed area (slot, pocket, recess). Tool spirals or zigzags inward to remove all material from an enclosed profile.
2D Contour — Cuts along the outside profile of a shape. Used to trim a part to its final outer boundary.
2D Adaptive Clearing — Smart roughing strategy that maintains a constant chip load by varying the engagement angle. Protects the tool and allows higher feed rates.
Face — Flattens the top surface of the stock. First operation in almost every job.
Drill — Peck drilling cycle for holes. Supports full drill cycles including peck depth, retract, and dwell.
Thread Milling — Helical path to cut internal or external threads using a thread mill cutter.

3D Operations (Complex Surfaces)

Adaptive Clearing 3D — Roughing for complex 3D shapes. Leaves even stock allowance all around the part.
Parallel — Finishing passes in parallel lines across a surface. Good for shallow, flat-ish surfaces.
Scallop — Maintains a consistent scallop (cusp) height across complex curved surfaces. More uniform surface finish than Parallel.
Contour — Follows the contour lines of a surface (like terrain elevation lines on a map). Good for steep walls.
Pencil — Traces concave corners and fillets with the tool tip. Used as a finishing pass to clean up corners that other strategies miss.
Radial — Passes radiate outward from a center point. Good for circular or radially symmetric parts.

CAM Simulation (Verify Before Cutting)

After creating toolpaths: Actions → Simulate

  • Watch the animated cutting simulation play through your entire toolpath
  • Check for gouges — areas where the tool cuts into geometry it shouldn't
  • Check for collisions — tool holder or spindle hitting the workpiece or fixture
  • Orange/red areas show material being removed as expected
  • Always simulate before sending G-code to a real machine

Post Processing — Generating G-Code

  1. Select your Setup in the browser
  2. Actions → Post Process
  3. Choose the correct post processor for your machine controller:
    • Grbl (hobby CNC, most 3-axis routers)
    • Fanuc (industrial mills)
    • Haas (Haas CNC machines)
    • Mach3 / Mach4 (hobby/semi-pro)
    • LinuxCNC (open-source controller)
  4. Set output folder and file name
  5. Click Post — output is a .nc or .gcode file
  6. Load file into your machine's controller software to run
Important: Always verify the G-code post processor matches your exact machine model. Wrong post processor = wrong coordinate system, wrong feeds, potential crash.

Design for Manufacturing (DFM)

DFM means designing parts so they can actually be manufactured efficiently and reliably. Each process has specific rules that must be followed.

Section A: Injection Molding Design Rules

Draft Angle — ALL vertical walls need 1–3° taper so the part can eject from the mold without sticking. Use the Draft feature in Fusion. Use Inspect → Draft Analysis to verify: green = good draft, red = no draft.
Uniform Wall Thickness — Keep wall thickness consistent, typically 1.5–4mm for most plastics. Thick sections cool slower and cause sink marks (depressions on the outer surface opposite a thick section).
Ribs — Add structural ribs to stiffen walls without adding bulk. Rib thickness = 60% of wall thickness. Maximum rib height = 3× wall thickness. Add draft to rib sides.
Bosses — Cylindrical posts for screws. Outer diameter = 2× screw diameter. Inner diameter = screw minor diameter + 0.4mm. Add gussets around bosses for strength.
Undercuts — Features that prevent the part from sliding out of the mold in the pull direction. Avoid them. If necessary, add side actions or lifters (significantly increases mold cost).
Parting Line — Where the two mold halves meet. Place it at the widest cross-section so both halves can release the part cleanly.
Gate Location — Where molten plastic enters the mold. Affects weld lines (where two flow fronts meet — a weakness) and sink marks. Gates are typically at the thickest section.
Fillet All Inside Corners — Sharp inside corners concentrate stress and restrict plastic flow. Add minimum 0.5mm fillet; 1mm+ preferred.
Quick check: Inspect → Draft Analysis. Run this before finalizing any injection-molded part. Every red face needs a draft angle added.

Section B: 3D Printing Design Rules (FDM)

Minimum Wall Thickness — 1.2mm minimum for FDM (= 3 perimeters at 0.4mm nozzle). Thinner walls may not print reliably.
Overhangs — Beyond 45° from horizontal requires support structures. Design parts to minimize overhangs where possible (orient parts smartly).
Bridging — Horizontal spans up to ~50mm can bridge without supports with good cooling and print settings. Beyond that, use supports or redesign.
Holes in Print Direction (Z-axis) — Tend to come out slightly oval due to layer stacking. Design 0.2mm oversize, or drill to final dimension after printing.
Tolerances for Fit:
  • Press fit: 0.2mm clearance (tight)
  • Clearance / slip fit: 0.4mm clearance (slides freely)
  • These values vary by printer calibration — test with a fit test print first
Orientation for Strength — FDM parts are weakest between layers (Z direction). Orient critical load paths in XY (within layers) for maximum strength.
Thread Inserts — Use heat-set threaded inserts (press in with a soldering iron) rather than printing threads for strong, reusable fastenings. M3/M4/M5 inserts are standard.
Embossed Text — Minimum 0.5mm relief height. Use bold sans-serif fonts for legibility (thin strokes may not print).
Thin Vertical Features — Pins thinner than 3mm diameter often snap during or after printing. Add fillets at the base and consider widening them.

Section C: CNC Machining Rules

Inside Corner Radii — CNC tools are round. Every inside corner will have a radius equal to (at least) the cutter radius. Design internal pockets with this radius in mind, or specify the radius to the machinist.
Deep Narrow Slots — Tool length limits how deep you can go. Keep depth:width ratio ≤ 6:1 for standard tooling.
Tall Thin Walls — Vibrate during machining, causing chatter and poor surface finish. Keep height:thickness ratio ≤ 8:1.
Design for Fixturing — The part must be held securely in a vise or fixture during cutting. Include flat parallel faces for clamping. Avoid features that make clamping impossible.
Through-Holes Preferred — Through-holes are faster and cheaper to machine than blind holes. Use blind holes only when structurally or functionally required.
Standard Drill Sizes — Design holes to standard drill bit sizes to avoid custom tooling cost. Reference ISO or ANSI drill size charts.
Surface Finish Specification:
  • Ra 3.2µm — as-machined (coarse, no secondary operation)
  • Ra 1.6µm — standard machined finish
  • Ra 0.8µm — fine finish (requires extra passes, costs more)
  • Ra 0.4µm and below — ground or polished (significant cost premium)

Section D: GD&T Basics (Geometric Dimensioning & Tolerancing)

GD&T is the standardized language for communicating precise dimensional requirements on engineering drawings.

Linear Tolerance

e.g. 25.00 ±0.1mm. Simple plus/minus on a dimension. Most common, but imprecise for location control.

Feature Control Frame

The rectangular box on a drawing containing: geometric symbol | tolerance value | datum references. Reads: "This feature must be within [tolerance] relative to datum [A, B, C]."

Datum Reference Frame

Three mutually perpendicular planes (A, B, C) that define the part's coordinate system. All GD&T tolerances reference these datums.

True Position

Controls the location of hole centers relative to datums. Uses a cylindrical tolerance zone. Critical for bolt patterns in assemblies.

Flatness

Surface must lie within two parallel planes separated by the tolerance value. No datum reference needed — self-contained.

Perpendicularity

A face or axis must be within a tolerance zone perpendicular to the reference datum. Common for mating surfaces.

Concentricity

The axis of a cylindrical feature must fall within a cylindrical tolerance zone coaxial with the datum axis.

In Fusion 360: Switch to the Drawing workspace → Annotate menu → GD&T symbols. You can add feature control frames, datum symbols, and surface finish callouts directly to your drawing views.

Practice Projects

These five projects are arranged in order of complexity. Complete them in sequence to build skills progressively. Each project introduces new tools and concepts.

Project 1 — Phone Stand Beginner

Skills: Sketching, Extrude, Fillet, basic constraints, User Parameters, STL export

  1. New design → User Parameters: BaseWidth=80mm, BaseDepth=60mm, Height=100mm, Angle=70deg, Thickness=5mm
  2. Create sketch on XY plane: draw rectangle (BaseWidth × BaseDepth) for the base footprint
  3. Extrude Thickness mm upward to create the base plate
  4. Create new sketch on the top face of the base: draw a line angled at the Angle parameter — this defines the phone groove direction
  5. Create the supporting back wall profile in a second sketch on the YZ plane — triangular cross-section to form the angled support
  6. Extrude the support sketch
  7. Create phone channel: sketch a slot shape on the front face, Extrude → Cut to create the groove that holds the phone edge
  8. Fillet all sharp edges: press F → select edges → 3mm radius
  9. Add chamfer to bottom edges for a clean, professional look
  10. Apply material appearance: matte black plastic
  11. Export: File → 3D Print → STL format for printing
Try changing the Angle parameter — the entire design updates automatically, demonstrating the power of parametric modeling.

Project 2 — Mounting Bracket Intermediate

Skills: Multiple features, mirroring, countersink holes, reinforcement ribs, simulation, drawing

  1. Parameters: Length=100mm, Width=60mm, Height=40mm, Thick=4mm
  2. Sketch base plate rectangle on XY plane, extrude Thick mm
  3. Sketch and extrude vertical wall (Height mm tall)
  4. Add Fillet at the base-wall junction: 8mm radius for structural strength
  5. Mounting holes: sketch 4 circles at corner positions (15mm from edges each direction), Extrude Cut → Through All
  6. Countersink for screw heads: Hole tool → Countersink, select the same face and positions
  7. Reinforcement rib: sketch a triangle between the base and wall on the side face, extrude (Thick×0.75 mm deep)
  8. Mirror the bracket half across the YZ plane to create a symmetric bracket
  9. Fillet all remaining sharp edges: 2mm radius
  10. Assign material: Aluminum 6061-T6
  11. Run Simulation: Fixed constraint on back wall faces, 500N downward on the base plate → check Safety Factor (target: 2.0+)
  12. Create Drawing: Drawing workspace → add Front, Top, Right, and Isometric views → add dimensions → fill in title block

Project 3 — Electronic Enclosure Intermediate–Advanced

Skills: Shell, Lip/Flange design, Screw bosses, PCB standoffs, Ventilation pattern, Assembly with Slider joint

  1. Parameters: BoxL=120mm, BoxW=80mm, BoxH=40mm, Wall=2.5mm, LidH=10mm
  2. Outer box: sketch rectangle, extrude (BoxL × BoxW × BoxH) solid rectangular body
  3. Shell feature: Shell → remove top face → Wall thickness → creates hollow box with open top
  4. Lid: create offset plane at BoxH−LidH height → sketch matching rectangle with 1mm inset → extrude LidH mm upward
  5. Lip for lid: sketch a small channel profile on the box top edge → extrude a flange inward so the lid seats on it
  6. Screw bosses: sketch circles at the 4 inside corners → extrude up from box bottom → add a hollowed center for the screw shaft
  7. PCB standoffs: 4 posts inside the box — 3mm diameter, M3 thread depth, positioned at standard PCB mounting spacing
  8. Ventilation slots: sketch a rectangular array on a side wall → Extrude Cut → Through All
  9. Cable grommet hole: circular hole in the rear wall, sized for your cable diameter + 0.5mm clearance
  10. Component panel cutout: sketch on the front face from reference positions for connector cutouts → Extrude Cut
  11. Assembly: make box and lid separate components → add Slider joint (lid translates straight up to open)
  12. Export both components as STL files for 3D printing
Check wall thickness uniformity with Inspect → Section Analysis before printing. Any thin spots in the shell will cause print failures.

Project 4 — Turbine Blade, Disk & Full Assembly Advanced

Skills: Loft, Fit Point Spline, offset planes, airfoil cross-sections, Circular Pattern, Component Pattern, Motion Study

Turbine Blade

  1. Parameters: BladeHeight=80mm, ChordRoot=30mm, ChordTip=18mm, TwistAngle=25deg
  2. Root profile sketch on XY plane: draw an elongated teardrop / leaf shape using Fit Point Spline. Width = ChordRoot, thickness ratio ~12% (3.6mm max thickness at 30% chord from leading edge). Leading edge = round, trailing edge = nearly pointed (tangent splines). Fully constrain the sketch.
  3. Create an offset plane at BladeHeight from XY plane
  4. Tip profile sketch on the new plane: smaller chord (ChordTip), rotated by TwistAngle relative to root profile. Same spline method — smaller and more twisted.
  5. Loft: Solid → Loft → select root profile, then tip profile. Add Z-axis centerline as a guide rail for smooth twist control.
  6. Root attachment: sketch T-slot or dovetail profile on the bottom of the blade → Extrude the root attachment shape → Mirror for symmetry
  7. Fillet leading/trailing edge transitions at the root-to-body junction: 2mm

Turbine Disk

  1. New component in the same design
  2. Sketch on XY plane: large outer circle OD=150mm, hub hole ID=30mm
  3. Extrude 20mm thick
  4. Fillet top and bottom edges of disk: 2mm
  5. Blade slot: Extrude Cut using the same T-slot / dovetail profile used on the blade root
  6. Circular Pattern: select the slot cut feature → Axis = Z axis through disk center → Quantity = 12 (for 12 blade slots)

Full Assembly

  1. Both blade and disk exist as components in the same design (or use Insert → Insert into Current Design to combine separate files)
  2. Ground the disk component (right-click → Ground)
  3. Add Joint: blade root → disk slot. Select Rigid joint (blade is fixed in disk slot)
  4. Circular Pattern of Components: Assemble → Pattern → Circular → select blade component → Z axis → 12 count (places 12 blades around the disk)
  5. Motion Study: add Revolute joint for the entire disk assembly → set rotation → play animation to see the turbine spinning
  6. Run Interference check (Inspect → Interference) to verify no blades overlap each other or the disk

Project 5 — Parametric Gear Advanced

Skills: Equation-driven parameters, involute tooth geometry, Circular Pattern, gear mating, Motion Link

  1. Parameters:
    • Module = 2mm (controls tooth size — larger module = larger teeth)
    • NumTeeth = 20
    • PressureAngle = 20deg
    • PitchDiameter = Module × NumTeeth = 40mm (calculated)
    • AddendumCircle = PitchDiameter + 2×Module = 44mm (tooth tip circle)
    • DedendumCircle = PitchDiameter − 2.5×Module = 35mm (tooth root circle)
  2. Sketch base circles: Pitch circle (construction line), Addendum circle (tooth tips), Dedendum circle (tooth roots)
  3. Involute tooth profile: approximate with arcs. For Module=2, tooth width at pitch circle ≈ π×Module/2 ≈ 3.14mm. Draw one full tooth from dedendum to addendum using the involute arc shape with a round root fillet.
  4. Circular Pattern: pattern the tooth profile × NumTeeth (20) around the center to complete the gear profile
  5. Extrude the full gear profile by 15mm
  6. Center hub hole: Extrude Cut → diameter = 8mm through the center
  7. Keyway: sketch a small rectangle on the hub hole interior → Extrude Cut for the shaft key slot
  8. Create a mating gear (same Module, different NumTeeth for different ratio). Position at correct center distance = Module×(Teeth1+Teeth2)/2
  9. Add Revolute joints to both gears. Add Motion Link between them with ratio = −Teeth1/Teeth2 (negative = opposite rotation direction, magnitude = gear ratio)
  10. Animate: drag one gear — the other rotates at the correct ratio and direction
Change the Module parameter and watch both gears automatically resize while maintaining proper mesh geometry — this is parametric design at its best.

Expert Knowledge

What Experts Do Differently

Plan Before Modeling

Sketch on paper first. Identify the key parametric relationships and feature order before opening Fusion.

Name Everything

Components, bodies, sketches, planes, parameters — use consistent, descriptive names. "Extrude12" tells you nothing; "BaseExtrude" does.

Parameters First

Create all key variables as User Parameters before creating the first sketch. Build the model by referencing these parameters, never hardcoded numbers.

Design Intent

Model features in the order they'd be manufactured — think like a machinist or toolmaker. The history tree should tell the manufacturing story.

Timeline Discipline

Keep the timeline clean. Use groups to organize related features. Suppress unused features during work to speed up computation.

Version Control

File → Version History → save named checkpoints before major changes. "Before_Shell_Op" is more useful than relying on undo history.

Design Table

Export parameter table, modify values in Excel, reimport — instantly generates design variants (different sizes, configurations) from one master model.

Test Early

Run interference checks and basic simulation at each design stage, not just at the end when changes are expensive and timeline is long.

Common Beginner Mistakes to Avoid

Over-constraining sketches — adding redundant dimensions that conflict. Fusion shows them red. Remove the conflicting dimension or constraint.
Under-constraining sketches — leaving sketch geometry blue (unconstrained). Blue geometry drifts when parent features are edited, causing unexpected model changes.
Mixing bodies and components haphazardly — Bodies are geometry; Components have their own coordinate system and work in assemblies. Use Components from the start for anything that will move, mate, or be assembled.
Not using parameters — Hardcoded dimensions make even simple design changes painful. Every key dimension should be a named parameter.
Adding fillets too early — Add all structural features (extrudes, cuts, ribs) first. Add fillets last, just before manufacturing. Fillets added mid-model complicate feature edits.
Boolean operations before features are finalized — Combining bodies early makes individual features hard to edit. Keep bodies separate until the design is finalized.
Ignoring draft analysis for injection-molded parts — Missing draft angles on a mold design means the part won't eject. Discovering this after tooling is cut costs tens of thousands of dollars.
Wrong material in simulation — Simulating steel but the part will be made from ABS plastic gives completely wrong safety factors. Always verify material assignment.
Not naming timeline steps — After editing a feature, right-click it in the timeline → Rename. "Cut12" vs "VentilationSlots" — one is useless three months later.
