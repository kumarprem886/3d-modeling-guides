Getting Started
Everything you need to buy, set up, and paint your very first miniature
The Camlin advantage: Camlin Artist Acrylics are water-based, fast-drying, and highly pigmented. While dedicated miniature paints (Citadel, Vallejo) are pre-thinned and ready to go, Camlin requires a little thinning and preparation — but costs a fraction of the price and the results are excellent once you know the tricks.
🛒 Essential Supply List

Paints (Camlin)

  • Ivory Black — primer base, shading, lining
  • Titanium White — highlighting, mixing, edge lights
  • Burnt Sienna — skin tones, leather, rust
  • Raw Umber — earth, brown shading, washes
  • Cadmium Red — armour, cloth, OSL
  • Phthalo Blue — space marines, sky, water
  • Sap Green — orks, foliage, cloth
  • Yellow Ochre — bone, sand, aged metal

Brushes

  • Size 1 round — basecoating large areas
  • Size 0 round — detail work, general use
  • Size 000 round — eyes, extreme detail
  • Flat brush (size 4) — drybrushing only
  • Old beaten brush — stippling, weathering
Indian brands to look for: Camlin, Brustro, or Da Vinci round brushes. Avoid synthetic flat brushes for detail — go for Kolinsky sable or good synthetic rounds.

🖥 Workspace Setup

Lighting — Most Important

  • Use a daylight bulb (5000–6500K) directly over your work — shadows kill detail visibility
  • Angle from the upper-left (like a studio light) so your brush hand doesn't cast a shadow
  • Avoid warm yellow household bulbs — they shift all your colours
  • A cheap LED ring light from Amazon works well
  • Never paint in dim light — you'll get poor consistency and miss details

Desk Setup

  • Paint standing or with the mini at eye level — not looking down
  • Keep two water jars — one dirty, one clean for mixing
  • Paper towels on the left, brushes on the right (or vice versa)
  • Use a wet palette — Camlin paints dry fast on a normal palette
  • Keep a magnifier lamp for extreme detail (eyes, insignia)
  • Have a white background card behind your mini to judge colours accurately
💧 DIY Wet Palette

A wet palette keeps Camlin acrylics workable for hours instead of minutes. Essential for blending and layering.

1
Get a shallow plastic container with lid

Any airtight container — old takeaway box, plastic tiffin box. Bigger is better.

2
Add a wet sponge or foam layer

Cut a kitchen sponge or foam to fit the bottom. Soak with water — squeeze out excess so it's damp, not dripping.

3
Lay baking paper (parchment paper) on top

Cut to fit. Baking/parchment paper is semi-permeable — water slowly seeps through keeping paint wet. Do NOT use regular paper or foil.

4
Place paint on the baking paper

Squeeze small dots of each Camlin colour. The moisture from below keeps them workable for 4–8 hours. Close the lid between sessions — paint stays fresh for days.

🧪 Thinning Camlin Paints — Critical Skill
Most common beginner mistake: Painting straight from the tube. Camlin acrylics from the tube are too thick — they obscure detail, leave brush strokes, and dry rough. Always thin your paint!

Consistency Guide

UseConsistencyMethod
BasecoatingSkimmed milk2–3 drops water per dab
LayeringWhole milk1–2 drops water per dab
WashesInk / watercolour5+ drops water, add flow improver
GlazingVery watery tint8–10 drops water, tiny pigment
DrybrushingAlmost no waterWipe brush almost completely dry

Flow Improver Recipe

Makes Camlin flow smoothly without breaking down pigment:

  • DIY: 10 parts water + 1 part Fevicol (PVA) — mix in a small bottle
  • Or: Vallejo Flow Improver — widely available in India
  • Or: Liquitex Flow Aid — best quality
  • Add 1–2 drops flow improver to your thinning water
  • Improves levelling, reduces brush marks, slows drying slightly
Brush test: Load your brush, then stroke it on the baking paper. You should get a smooth, even coat that covers without pooling. If it beads up = too thick. If it runs and shows no pigment = too thin.

How to use Camlin's range specifically for miniature painting

⚗ Making Camlin Washes & Inks
Washes are the single most impactful skill. A good wash applied over a basecoat instantly adds depth, shadow, and realism with almost zero effort.

Basic Wash Formula

Mix in a small jar or bottle cap:

  • 1 part Camlin paint
  • 5–8 parts water
  • 1 drop Fevicol / PVA (prevents tide marks)
  • 1 drop flow improver (optional)
  • 1 drop dishwashing liquid (breaks surface tension)

Test on a spare mini first — adjust ratio until it flows into recesses without pooling on flat areas.

Camlin Wash Recipes

  • Brown wash (universal): Burnt Umber + Burnt Sienna 3:1 diluted 1:7
  • Black wash (metal/dark): Ivory Black diluted 1:8
  • Green wash (orks/flesh): Sap Green 1:7
  • Blue wash (NMM cool): Phthalo Blue 1:8
  • Sepia wash (skin/bone): Raw Umber + Burnt Sienna 2:1 diluted 1:7
  • Purple wash (gems): Dioxazine Purple 1:6
✨ Camlin Metallics — Getting the Best Result

Camlin metallics have large metallic flake particles — they can look grainy at small scale. Tips to fix this:

  • Thin with very little water — too much water separates flakes
  • Apply in thin coats, let dry, apply again — builds up coverage
  • For gold: basecoat in Burnt Sienna first, then gold on top — richer result
  • For silver: basecoat in dark grey (Payne's Grey), then silver
  • Always finish metallics with a gloss varnish — makes them shine properly
  • Mix a little Ivory Black into silver for "dark" steel
  • Mix gold + silver for aged bronze effect

Metallic Layering Order

1
Dark basecoat

Burnt Sienna (gold) or Payne's Grey (silver) in recesses

2
Metallic layer

Apply main metallic paint leaving dark areas in recesses

3
Wash

Brown or black wash in recesses — deepens shadows dramatically

4
Edge highlight

Pure silver/gold on sharp edges only — catchlights

Color Theory for Miniatures
Understanding colour makes every mini look more professional — even with simple techniques
🌈 The Colour Wheel

Primary Colours (Camlin)

  • Red — Cadmium Red
  • Blue — Phthalo Blue or Ultramarine
  • Yellow — Cadmium Yellow or Yellow Ochre

Secondary Colours (mix from primaries)

  • Orange = Red + Yellow
  • Purple = Red + Blue
  • Green = Blue + Yellow

Warm vs Cool

  • Warm colours: Reds, oranges, yellows — appear to advance (come forward)
  • Cool colours: Blues, purples, greens — appear to recede (go back)
  • Use warm colours for highlights, cool for shadows — creates life-like depth
  • Example: flesh shadow = add purple/blue, flesh highlight = add yellow/orange
🎭 Colour Schemes for Miniatures
Monochromatic

One colour in different shades and tints. Easy to execute, always looks cohesive. Add black to shade, white to highlight. Great for Chaos undivided, stone statues.

Complementary

Opposite colours on the wheel (Red + Green, Blue + Orange, Purple + Yellow). Maximum contrast. Use one as primary, the other as accent. Classic for vibrant minis.

Triadic

Three equally-spaced colours. Red + Blue + Yellow. Bold and vibrant. Use one dominant, one secondary, one accent. Hard to execute but striking.

Analogous

Three neighbouring colours (Blue + Blue-Green + Green). Natural, harmonious look. Great for nature-themed minis, Sylvaneth, Nurgle.

Split Complementary

One colour + two adjacent to its complement. Softer than full complementary. More interesting than analogous. E.g. Blue + Red-Orange + Yellow-Orange.

Neutral with Pop

Mostly grey/brown/bone with one strong accent colour. Very cinematic look. Ideal for modern sci-fi minis — grey armour, red lenses, gold trim.

💡 Value — Light and Shadow
Value is more important than colour. A mini painted in greyscale with good light and shadow will look better than a colourful mini with flat, even paint. Train your eye to see value first.

The 5-Value System

  • Deep shadow — recesses, underneath, behind — 10–20% brightness
  • Shadow — sides, mid-lower areas — 30–40%
  • Midtone (basecoat) — main colour — 50%
  • Highlight — raised edges, top surfaces — 65–75%
  • Specular highlight — sharpest edges only — 90–100%

Mixing Shades and Highlights

  • Shade: Add Ivory Black or Payne's Grey to your base colour (not pure black — it looks dead)
  • Cool shade: Add Phthalo Blue or Dioxazine Purple for a cool shadow
  • Highlight: Add Titanium White or Naples Yellow to your base colour
  • Warm highlight: Add Yellow Ochre or touch Cadmium Yellow for warm light
  • Never mix pure black/white — always add to the base colour

Prep & Priming
Good prep is half the paintjob — skip this and all your work peels off
Never skip priming. Acrylic paint (including Camlin) does not bond to bare plastic, resin, or metal. Primer creates a micro-textured surface for paint to grip. Without it, paint chips, peels, and slides within hours.
🔧 Assembly & Cleaning
1
Remove mold lines

Run a hobby knife or the back edge of a blade gently along every join seam — the mold line appears as a faint ridge. Scrape, don't cut. Then smooth with 400-grit sandpaper. Mold lines show up horribly after painting.

2
Remove flash (thin plastic film)

Carefully clip off any thin film or sprue remnants with clippers. Clean with knife if needed.

3
Wash the model in warm soapy water

Scrub gently with an old toothbrush. Rinse thoroughly, pat dry. This removes mold release agents that prevent paint adhesion. Especially important for resin models.

4
Assemble strategically

Consider leaving some parts separate (arms, backpack) to access hard-to-reach areas. Glue these after painting. Use Fevi-Kwik (superglue) for plastic-to-plastic; add baking soda to fill gaps.

5
Mount on a painting handle

Blu-tack the model to a bottle cap, cork, or dedicated painting handle. Never hold the model directly — skin oils contaminate paint, and finger grease creates a barrier.

Black Primer — When to Use

  • Dark armies (Chaos, Undead, Dark Eldar)
  • Armoured metallic minis
  • Quick batch painting — shadows are already done
  • Beginners — mistakes are less visible on dark primer

Grey Primer — Recommended Default

  • Works for any colour scheme
  • Easier to judge paint consistency than black
  • Neither too bright nor too dark
  • Best for Zenithal priming technique

White Primer — When to Use

  • Bright colour schemes (white armour, yellow, light blue)
  • Speed painting with washes directly over primer
  • Showing off translucent paint effects
  • Harder to control — mistakes stand out more
🌟 Zenithal Priming — Instant Depth

The single best technique to instantly add light-and-shadow before painting even starts.

1
Black prime the whole model

Full coverage black spray from all angles. This fills every shadow area automatically.

2
Grey prime from 45°

Hold spray at 45° above the model. Light mist only — grey should hit the sides.

3
White prime from directly above

Spray white from directly above — only the tops of raised surfaces get white. Result: a perfect light-to-shadow gradient before any paint touches the brush.

After zenithal priming, apply a thin wash over the whole model — it will settle into the pre-existing shadows and create instant realism. Then just add basecolours and light highlights. This technique halves painting time.
Painting Techniques
Every major technique explained — when to use it and how to execute it with Camlin paints
🖌 Basecoating Beginner

Applying the first solid layer of colour over primer. Should achieve full, even coverage in 1–2 thin coats.

Camlin tip: Thin to skimmed-milk consistency. Two thin coats always beat one thick coat. Let each coat dry fully (5–10 min) before the next.

Goal: even coverage, no streaks, no obscured detail
💧 Washing / Shading Beginner

Applying a very thin, dark paint mixture that flows into recesses by gravity and surface tension. Instantly adds depth and shadow.

Camlin tip: Use the brown wash recipe (Burnt Umber 1:7 + drop of Fevicol). Apply generously over the basecoat and let gravity do the work. Don't brush it around — let it flow.

Let it dry completely before the next step (20–30 min)
🔺 Drybrushing Beginner

Load a flat brush with paint, wipe almost all of it off on paper, then lightly drag it over raised surfaces. Paint catches only on the highest points.

Camlin tip: Use undiluted or barely thinned paint. Keep wiping until brush leaves almost nothing on paper, then flick across textured areas. Perfect for fur, hair, stone, rust.

Use a separate dedicated flat brush — drybrushing ruins good brushes
🎭 Layering Intermediate

Building up highlights in progressively lighter colours, each coat applied over a smaller area than the last. Creates smooth, controlled gradients.

Camlin tip: Thin to whole-milk consistency. Each layer should be transparent enough to see the previous one through it. 3–5 layers typical for quality work.

Work from dark to light, smaller area each time
🌊 Wet Blending Advanced

Placing two wet colours side by side and blending the transition while both are still wet. Creates seamless, smooth colour transitions.

Camlin tip: Add a tiny drop of retarder medium or glycerin to slow drying. Work quickly. Use a damp brush to blend the middle zone. Camlin's thick pigment holds well for wet blending.

Work small areas at a time — paint dries in seconds on small surfaces
🔮 Glazing Intermediate

Applying very thin transparent washes of colour to tint or shift hues without obscuring the detail below. Like coloured glass over a surface.

Camlin tip: Dilute 1:10 or more. The paint should be barely tinted water. Build up 5–10 thin glazes for subtle colour shifts. Great for skin and blending transitions.

Add flow improver — helps spread perfectly without streaks
📍 Edge Highlighting Intermediate

Painting a thin bright line only on the sharpest edges of armour panels, cloaks, and raised detail. Creates a "comic book" style contrast.

Camlin tip: Use your finest round brush (000). Load minimal paint, drag the very tip along the edge at a shallow angle. Keep lines thin and consistent — confidence in stroke matters more than speed.

Practice on card before touching the mini
✏ Line / Recess Shading Intermediate

Carefully drawing a thin dark line inside panel recesses and joins with a fine brush. More controlled than a wash — applies shade exactly where you want.

Camlin tip: Thin Ivory Black or Burnt Umber to an ink consistency. Use the tip of a 000 brush. Draw the brush along the recess like writing. Patience — go slow and steady.

Correct with the base colour if you overshoot
🌀 Stippling Intermediate

Dabbing with an almost-dry brush to create texture — skin pores, rough stone, weathered leather, rust spots.

Camlin tip: Use an old frayed brush with minimal paint. Dab in circular or random patterns. Layer multiple colours for realistic rust or aged surfaces.

Great for quick texture on large surfaces like bases
💥 Sponge Weathering Beginner

Dabbing with a torn blister foam/sponge piece to create random chipping patterns on armour.

Camlin tip: Tear the sponge for an irregular edge. Dab Ivory Black first (deep chip), then Burnt Sienna (rust inside chip), then Silver (fresh metal edge).

Less is more — one or two dabs per panel looks realistic
🌈 Two-Brush Blending Advanced

One brush applies paint, a second clean damp brush immediately blends and softens the edges. Achieves smooth transitions without a retarder medium.

Camlin tip: Keep the second brush damp (not wet). Apply a stroke, immediately soften the hard edge with the second brush. Work fast — Camlin dries in 30–60 seconds on miniature-scale surfaces.

Wet palette essential — keeps paint workable longer
🔥 Freehanding Expert

Painting freehand designs — chapter symbols, squad markings, murals on cloaks, insignia on banners — directly on the mini.

Camlin tip: Sketch lightly with a watered-down Ivory Black first (easily corrected). Work in layers: outline → fill → clean up edges with base colour → refine. Good lighting and magnification essential.

Sketch on paper first, then transfer confidence to the mini
Learning order recommendation: Basecoat → Wash → Drybrush → Layer → Edge highlight → Glaze → Wet blend → Freehand. Master each technique on 2–3 minis before moving to the next. Don't rush advanced techniques — value from basics beats poor execution of advanced ones.
Complete Painting Workflow
Step-by-step from bare plastic to display-ready miniature — three quality levels
⚡ Speed Paint (1–2 hours per mini) Batch Painting
Perfect for painting large armies quickly. Looks great on the table at arm's length.
1
Prime in grey spray

Zenithal if you have 10 extra minutes — adds instant depth for free.

2
Basecoat all major areas

Thin Camlin paint to skimmed milk. Block in armour, cloth, skin, weapons — one colour per area, don't worry about neatness yet.

3
Apply a brown wash over everything

Burnt Umber 1:7 wash generously applied. Let it flow into all recesses. Let dry completely.

4
Drybrush highlights

Take the basecolour + add white. Drybrush raised areas. Fast and effective.

5
Tidy edges, add eyes

Clean up any accidents. Paint eyes: black, then tiny white dot in the corner. Don't overthink eyes at this level.

6
Base the model, varnish

Quick texture paste or PVA + sand on base, paint it. Matte varnish the whole model. Done.

🎨 Standard Paint (4–8 hours per mini) Display Quality
Great balance of quality and time. Looks stunning up close. Your go-to for characters and centrepiece models.
1
Prep & prime

Remove all mold lines, wash model, assemble. Grey spray prime. Zenithal highlight with white from above.

2
Basecoat in thin layers

2–3 thin coats per colour area. Full even coverage. Let each layer dry. Mix colours to match your chosen scheme.

3
Shade with washes

Apply targeted washes per area — brown wash on skin/leather, black wash on metal, colour-appropriate wash on armour. Don't use one wash on everything.

4
Layer highlights

Mix base colour + 30% white. Apply to raised areas avoiding deepest recesses. Repeat with 60% white on even smaller areas. 2–3 highlight layers per surface.

5
Edge highlight

Near-white on the sharpest edges of armour panels and raised details. Thin line only — don't flood the edge.

6
Details: eyes, gems, metallics

Eyes: black → bright colour → white dot. Gems: dark at top, bright at bottom, tiny white speck. Metallics: layer and edge highlight.

7
Base and varnish

Detailed base — multiple layers, tufts, rocks. Gloss varnish on lenses/gems, matte on everything else.

🏆 Competition Quality (20–40+ hours) Display Masterpiece
Maximum effort for single showcase pieces. Every surface blended smoothly, every detail pushed to its limit.
  1. Perfect prep — every mold line removed, gap-filled with putty, re-sculpted if needed
  2. Zenithal prime with airbrush for ultra-smooth gradient base
  3. Pre-shade recesses with thinned Ivory Black or deep colour mix
  4. Basecoat with airbrush or many thin brush layers for zero brush strokes
  5. Wet blend all major surfaces — smooth gradients across every panel
  6. Multiple glaze layers to deepen colour richness and adjust temperature
  7. 5–7 highlight layers per surface — each smaller and brighter
  8. Recess shade every panel line with thinned dark colour
  9. NMM on metal surfaces — no metallic paint, pure colour gradients
  10. OSL — coloured light from glowing sources bleeds onto surrounding surfaces
  11. Freehand designs on banners, cloaks, armour panels
  12. Eyes, gems, teeth, tongues — each its own micro-painting session
  13. Sculpted or premium resin base — detailed and painted to same standard
  14. Strategic varnish — gloss on wet surfaces, satin on skin, matte on cloth, gloss on lenses
Advanced Techniques
OSL, NMM, skin, eyes — the techniques that separate good painters from great ones
💡 OSL — Object Source Lighting

Painting the coloured light cast by glowing elements (plasma coils, lenses, fire, runes) onto surrounding surfaces.

OSL Rules

  • Light falls on surfaces facing the source — not behind or occluded
  • Intensity decreases with distance — stronger near the source
  • Light colour desaturates and lightens as it spreads
  • Surfaces cast hard shadows blocking light beyond them
  • The source itself should be the brightest point on the model

OSL with Camlin — Step by Step

  1. Paint the normal basecoat and shading on all surfaces first
  2. Identify which surfaces face the glow source
  3. Mix light source colour + white (e.g. Phthalo Blue + white for blue plasma)
  4. Glaze it onto facing surfaces — most concentrated nearest source
  5. Add pure white to the glaze and apply an even smaller area right next to source
  6. The source itself: near-white centre, full colour mid, dark edge
  7. Add a gloss varnish on the source element for extra glow effect
Common OSL sources on Warhammer minis: Plasma guns (blue/teal), power swords (blue-white), chainswords (red-orange), daemon eyes (green/red), neon runes (purple), fire (orange-yellow).
⚙ NMM — Non-Metallic Metal

Painting the appearance of metal using only matt colours — no metallic paint. Requires understanding how metal reflects light.

How Metal Behaves

  • Metal reflects its surroundings like a mirror — the sky above, ground below
  • High contrast — very bright highlights and very dark shadows, close together
  • Transition from near-black to near-white in millimetres
  • The brightest spot is a tiny sharp "specular" highlight — almost pure white
  • Dark areas often reflect the complementary colour of the light source

Gold NMM with Camlin

  • Darkest: Burnt Umber + Black
  • Dark mid: Burnt Sienna + Raw Umber
  • Mid: Yellow Ochre + Burnt Sienna
  • Highlight: Yellow Ochre + Cadmium Yellow
  • Brightest: Titanium White + touch Cadmium Yellow
  • Blend each step smoothly into the next
  • Specular: pure white dot on the sharpest corner

Silver NMM with Camlin

  • Darkest: Ivory Black + Phthalo Blue
  • Dark: Payne's Grey
  • Mid: Neutral Grey
  • Highlight: White + small Neutral Grey
  • Specular: Pure Titanium White
NMM tip: Tape a silver spoon to your lamp and study where the dark and light areas fall. NMM is about accurate observation — where is the sky reflection? Where is the shadow from below? Paint what you see, not what you know.
👁 Painting Eyes
Eyes make or break a mini. Small and difficult — but the first thing anyone looks at. Worth spending 15 extra minutes getting right.
1
Fill the eye socket black

Ivory Black into the entire eye cavity. This becomes the pupil and the outline all in one.

2
Paint the white

Titanium White (+ tiny touch Payne's Grey for off-white) — fill the eye from the bottom leaving a black "lid line" at the top. Slightly below the top of the socket.

3
Add the iris (optional for small minis)

A dot of colour (brown, blue, green) in the centre of the white area. For 28mm scale, just black + white is often enough.

4
Specular highlight

A single tiny dot of pure white in the upper corner of the eye (upper right for both eyes). Creates the "alive" look.

5
Clean up

Use the skin base colour on a 000 brush to neaten the borders of the eye. Fix any accidents carefully.

🩺 Painting Skin

Human Skin (Medium tone) — Camlin

  • Base: Burnt Sienna + Titanium White + Yellow Ochre 3:2:1
  • Shadow: Base + Burnt Sienna + touch Dioxazine Purple
  • Deep shadow: Raw Umber + Purple
  • Mid highlight: Base + more White + Yellow Ochre
  • High highlight: Near-white + tiny Yellow Ochre
  • Blush: Cadmium Red very diluted glazed on cheeks, nose, ears
  • 5 o'clock shadow: Phthalo Blue + Dioxazine Purple + white glaze on jaw

Dark Skin Tone

  • Base: Burnt Umber + Raw Umber 2:1
  • Shadow: + Ivory Black
  • Highlight: + Burnt Sienna + Yellow Ochre
  • Bright highlight: + Naples Yellow + White
  • Specular highlights on nose, brow, cheekbones are brighter contrast

Ork / Alien Skin

  • Base: Sap Green + Yellow Ochre 2:1
  • Shadow: Sap Green + Ivory Black
  • Highlight: + Cadmium Yellow
💎 Painting Gemstones
1
Basecoat in mid-tone colour

E.g. Phthalo Blue for a blue gem, Cadmium Red for red.

2
Dark top half

Mix base + Ivory Black. Fill the upper half of the gem — this is the shadow where no light enters from below.

3
Bright lower half

Mix base + Titanium White. Fill the lower half — this is the light transmitted through the gem.

4
Specular highlight

Tiny pure white dot in the upper-left corner of the gem — this is the direct light reflection.

5
Gloss varnish

A tiny dot of gloss varnish on top — makes gems look wet and glass-like.

Basing & Finishing
The base completes the mini — a great base elevates even an average paintjob
🌿 Basing Materials (Available in India)
Sand / Fine Grit

Regular building sand or aquarium sand. PVA glue to base, let dry, paint brown then drybrush tan/bone. The most versatile base texture.

Dried and pressed autumn leaves, scaled down, look incredibly realistic on autumn forest bases.

🔮 Varnishing — Protecting Your Work
Always varnish your minis. Acrylic paint — especially Camlin — chips easily during handling and gaming. Varnish protects weeks of work. It also fixes frosting, corrects sheen inconsistencies, and makes the model look intentional.

Varnish Types

  • Gloss — shiny, strong protection, great under washes
  • Satin — semi-sheen, good for skin and leather
  • Matte — flat/no shine, the final coat for most minis

Available in India

  • Rustoleum 2X Matte Clear — hardware stores
  • Vallejo Matte Varnish — brush-on, excellent
  • Citadel Munitorum Varnish — spray, premium
  • Mod Podge Matte — craft stores, works well

Varnishing Workflow

  1. Let mini dry completely — 24 hours is ideal
  2. Apply gloss varnish first — seals the paint and provides a smooth surface for the matte coat
  3. Let gloss dry fully (1–2 hours)
  4. Apply matte varnish — thin even coats
  5. After matte: apply gloss selectively on gems, lenses, wet surfaces
  6. Never spray varnish in humid conditions (above 70% RH) — causes frosting
Fix frosted varnish: Brush-on gloss varnish over frosted areas — it remelts the surface and removes the white haze. Then re-apply matte on top.

💡 Pro Tips — The Things Nobody Tells You
Less paint, more passes

Two thin coats always beat one thick coat. Every time. No exceptions. Thin your paint more than you think you need to.

Wipe brush before loading

After rinsing, always wipe on a paper towel before dipping in paint again. Excess water dilutes your paint unpredictably.

Rotate the mini, not the brush

Turn the mini to a comfortable angle for each stroke. Don't try to paint upside-down or awkwardly — always put your brush in its strongest direction.

Paint eye level

Hold the mini at eye level when painting faces, eyes, and fine detail. Looking down distorts perspective and causes poor placement.

Dark before light always

Always paint darks first, then midtones, then lights. Correcting a bright area with dark paint is much harder — the dark must go over many coats to cover.

Take photos to see mistakes

Phone camera sees things your eye misses. Photograph under bright light every few sessions to spot patchy coverage, missed areas, and proportion issues.

One focal point

The face is always the focal point. Spend 40% of your time on the face and make everything else support it. Over-detailed armour competing with a plain face looks wrong.

Consistent light source

Decide your light comes from above-left and never change it. All highlights on top-left surfaces, all shadows on bottom-right. Inconsistent lighting looks amateur.

Dettol stripping

Submerge a badly painted mini in Dettol (brown antiseptic) for 12 hours, then scrub with old toothbrush. Removes all paint cleanly — back to bare plastic. Start fresh.

Compare to references

Keep reference photos of real miniatures next to your work. Your brain adjusts to what it sees — a fresh reference resets your eye and reveals where to improve.

Finished is better than perfect

A completed mini on the table beats a perfect mini never finished. Perfection leads to abandonment. Paint to done, not to ideal.

Watch while you paint

Put on YouTube painting tutorials while you paint. Passively absorbing techniques for hours while practicing them is the fastest way to improve.

📚 Recommended Learning Path
1
Month 1: Learn the basics on cheap minis

Buy a cheap box (Night Goblins, Skeleton Warriors, or Contrast paints starter set). Paint 10 using just basecoat + wash + drybrush. Focus on getting comfortable, not perfect results.

2
Month 2: Add layering & edge highlighting

Pick 3 minis from your first batch. Re-paint them with added layering on armour and edge highlights. Compare before/after — the improvement will be dramatic.

3
Month 3: Tackle a character or hero model

Spend a week on one mini. Apply everything learned. Face, eyes, details, proper basing. This is your benchmark — photograph it for your "first real mini."

4
Month 4–6: Learn wet blending & glazing

Dedicate sessions to just practicing the technique on spare models. Don't try to rush a display piece while still learning wet blending — practice first.

5
Month 6+: Attempt OSL or NMM

These are advanced techniques. Try OSL on a mini with a plasma gun or power sword. Don't aim for perfection first time — understanding the concept is the goal.
