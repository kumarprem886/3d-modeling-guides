Understanding LaserGRBL

LaserGRBL is a completely free, open-source Windows application that sends G-code instructions to GRBL-based laser engravers and cutters. It handles image conversion, job control, and real-time machine communication — all without ever asking for a payment. No watermarks, no file limits, no expiry.

What Is LaserGRBL?

LaserGRBL was created by Diego Manzoli (GitHub: arkypita) and first released around 2016. Written in C#, it runs natively on Windows and communicates with laser machines over USB serial. The software converts raster images (photos, logos, line art) and vector files (SVG) into G-code streams that GRBL firmware can execute to move the laser head precisely.

Unlike proprietary alternatives that lock features behind paywalls, LaserGRBL gives you access to every feature from day one — dithering algorithms, engraving direction, speed/power profiles, G-code preview, camera overlay, and more. The source code lives on GitHub at arkypita/LaserGRBL where the community can audit, fork, and contribute.

What Is GRBL?

GRBL (pronounced "gerbil") is open-source firmware that runs on an Arduino Uno or Nano (ATmega328P) and transforms G-code commands into stepper motor pulses. Nearly every budget diode laser under ₹40,000 uses a GRBL-based control board. When your computer sends G1 X50 Y50 F3000 S800, GRBL interprets that as "move to X=50mm Y=50mm at 3000mm/min with laser at 800/1000 power."

GRBL Key Parameters

  • $30 — Maximum S-value (usually 1000)
  • $32 — Laser mode enable (set to 1)
  • $110/$111 — Max feed rate X/Y (mm/min)
  • $120/$121 — Acceleration X/Y
  • $100/$101 — Steps per mm X/Y

GRBL Versions You'll Encounter

  • GRBL 1.1f/h — Most common on machines sold 2020–present
  • GRBL-LPC — 32-bit variant (rare on budget machines)
  • Sculpfun/Ortur custom — GRBL 1.1 with small vendor tweaks

Setup & Connection

Getting LaserGRBL to talk to your laser is usually simple — plug in USB, select the right COM port and baud rate, click Connect. This tab covers every step and every problem you might hit, especially common India-specific issues with CH340 USB chips found on most Chinese laser boards.

Step 1: Install the CH340 Driver

Most Chinese laser control boards (Sculpfun, Atomstack, Ortur budget lines, generic A5/A20 boards) use the CH340G or CH341 USB-to-serial chip because it costs about ₹5 vs ₹80 for the FTDI chip. Windows 10/11 sometimes installs it automatically, but it often doesn't, causing the machine to simply not appear as a COM port.

  1. Plug in your laser via USB. Open Device Manager (Win + X → Device Manager). Look under "Ports (COM & LPT)". If you see "CH340" or "USB-SERIAL CH340" — the driver is already installed. If you see a yellow warning triangle or nothing at all — you need the driver.
  2. Download the CH340 driver from wch-ic.com/downloads/CH341SER_EXE.html (official WCH website). This is the Chinese manufacturer's site — it's safe and the correct source.
  3. Run CH341SER.EXE as Administrator. Click INSTALL. You'll see a "Driver install success" popup in about 3 seconds.
  4. Unplug and re-plug the USB cable. Device Manager should now show USB-SERIAL CH340 (COM3) or similar. Note the COM number — you'll need it in LaserGRBL.
  5. If using Ortur Laser Master 3 or newer Sculpfun machines, they may use a CP2102 chip instead. Download "CP210x Universal Windows Driver" from Silicon Labs website. Same install process.
Common India Problem: "No COM Port Found"
  • Try a different USB cable — many included cables are charge-only with no data wires. This is extremely common with Indian bundled accessories. Use a cable that has USB data (test it with your phone file transfer).
  • Plug directly into the PC, not through a USB hub. Hubs can cause recognition failures especially on older motherboards.
  • If using Windows 7: You may need the older CH340 driver version 3.4 instead of the latest — check the WCH driver archive.
  • Some counterfeit CH340 chips need a specific "patched" driver — search "CH340 driver fake chip fix" if none of the above works.

Step 3: Verify GRBL Firmware with $$ Command

In the console input box at the bottom of LaserGRBL, type $$ and press Enter. This dumps all GRBL configuration parameters. Key things to check:

Must Verify: $32 Laser Mode

$32=1

This must be 1 for laser operation. If it's 0 (spindle mode), the laser will fire at full power the moment it moves, ruining your material. Type $32=1 in the console and press Enter to fix it.

Must Verify: $30 Max S-Value

$30=1000

This defines the maximum laser power value. LaserGRBL needs to know this to correctly translate percentage power to S-values. In LaserGRBL settings (menu → Settings), set "Laser power max" to match your $30 value.

Mismatch Warning: $30 and LaserGRBL Setting

If GRBL has $30=255 but LaserGRBL thinks it's 1000, your "100% power" in LaserGRBL will actually send S=1000 when the machine only accepts up to S=255 — which may cause unpredictable behavior. Always match these. Most modern machines use $30=1000. Some older ones use $30=255. Run $$ and check.

Step 4: Homing and Workspace Setup

Most budget diode lasers do not have limit switches / homing switches (they would cost extra). This means your laser has no "home" position — it powers on wherever the head happens to be. This affects how LaserGRBL handles the origin.

Machines Without Limit Switches (Most Budget Lasers)

  1. Physically move the laser head to the bottom-left corner of the work area (or wherever you want the origin to be) by hand — while the machine is powered off, or using the jog controls after connecting.
  2. In LaserGRBL, click the "Set Zero" button (looks like a target/crosshair). This tells GRBL: "this current position = 0,0." Now all job coordinates are relative to this point.
  3. Before every job, always re-verify the laser head is at your intended origin. LaserGRBL will not move it there automatically.

Machines With Limit Switches (Sculpfun S30 Pro, some Ortur)

  1. In the LaserGRBL console, type $H and press Enter. The machine will home — moving to the corner with limit switches at high speed, then slowly backing off for precise positioning.
  2. After homing, position 0,0 is defined by firmware. All your jobs will be absolute from that corner.
  3. If you get a "GRBL Alarm: Homing fail" error, check that the limit switch wires are seated properly in their connectors on the control board.

Unlock After Alarm State

GRBL enters Alarm state after power-on or after an error (limit switch triggered, etc.). The console will show ALARM:1 or similar. Type $X in the console to unlock and resume normal operation. Never force-move the machine while in alarm state.

First Connection Troubleshooting Checklist

Systematic Diagnosis
  • No COM port in list: CH340 driver not installed → see Step 1
  • COM port shown but won't connect: Wrong baud rate, or another program (Pronterface, Arduino IDE Serial Monitor) has the port open — close them
  • Connects but console blank: USB cable is charge-only, no data lines — try different cable
  • Console shows gibberish: Wrong baud rate — try 9600 instead of 115200 (old machine)

Control Panel (Right Side)

Position Display

Shows current X, Y coordinates in mm. These are work coordinates (relative to your set zero point), not machine coordinates. If GRBL reports machine position (MPos), LaserGRBL converts it automatically.

Jog Controls

The arrow buttons move the laser head. The dropdown next to them sets the jog step size — typically 0.1mm, 1mm, 10mm, 100mm. Hold Shift while clicking for continuous movement (only on some LaserGRBL versions). Jog commands are sent as real-time GRBL jog commands ($J= protocol) so they can be cancelled instantly.

Never Jog Past Machine Limits

Budget diode lasers have no limit switches (usually). If you jog X or Y beyond the physical travel range, the stepper motor will skip steps and you will lose your origin position. Always jog slowly near the edges, and watch the machine physically to see when it hits the end of travel.

Set Zero / Go to Zero

  • Set Zero — Defines current position as the job origin (0,0). Do this before every job.
  • Go to Zero — Moves the laser head back to the defined origin. Useful for re-running a job on the same material.

Laser Test Fire

The Test Fire button (lightbulb icon, usually at very low power ~1–5%) fires the laser briefly so you can check focus or mark the exact center of a material. Configure the test fire power in Settings — never leave it at full power. Test fire should be visible as a dim dot on your material without burning.

Job Progress

During a job, the progress bar shows percentage complete. The time estimate is calculated from job G-code and can be inaccurate on first few jobs — LaserGRBL learns from actual feed rates. You'll also see a live lines-per-second counter showing how fast GRBL is processing commands.

Preview Area

The large central area shows your loaded image/G-code as a preview. Key interactions:

  • Mouse scroll — Zoom in/out
  • Right-click drag — Pan the preview
  • Left-click on preview — In some modes, moves the job origin to clicked position
  • Red border rectangle — Represents your machine's work area (set in Settings → Work Area)
  • Blue/green job outline — The actual job boundaries. Should fit inside the red border.
Setting Work Area Dimensions

In Settings, enter your machine's actual cutting area dimensions (e.g., 410×420 for Sculpfun S9). This draws the boundary rectangle in the preview and prevents you from accidentally running a job that would exceed the machine's travel range. Without this set, LaserGRBL has no way to warn you about out-of-bounds jobs.

Image Engraving

LaserGRBL's core strength is converting raster images — photos, logos, line art — into laser engravable G-code. The conversion wizard gives you control over dithering algorithm, line density, direction, and size. Understanding these settings is the difference between a muddy, patchy burn and a sharp, detailed engraving.

The Image Import Workflow

  1. Click File → Open File (or the folder icon, or drag & drop onto the preview area). Select a PNG, JPG, BMP, or GIF file.
  2. The Image Conversion Wizard opens. This is LaserGRBL's most important dialog — don't rush through it.
  3. Set your target size in the Width/Height fields (in mm). Enable "Keep Aspect Ratio" to avoid distortion.
  4. Choose your Conversion Algorithm (dithering method — see below). The preview updates in real time.
  5. Set Line Distance (controls resolution/DPI). Set Engraving Direction (horizontal, vertical, diagonal).
  6. Set Speed and Power (S-value) for the job.
  7. Click Next. The G-code is generated and loaded into the preview. You can still adjust position by clicking in the preview.
  8. Click Run to start engraving, or Burn Border first to trace the job outline at low power and verify placement.

Supported Image Formats

  • JPEG (.jpg/.jpeg) — Most common; good for photos
  • PNG (.png) — Best for logos, supports transparency (transparent areas = no laser fire)
  • BMP — Windows bitmap, works well
  • GIF — Supported, but use PNG instead
  • TIFF — Supported in newer versions

For best results, pre-process your image in MS Paint, GIMP, or Photoshop to adjust contrast/brightness before importing. LaserGRBL's built-in adjustments are basic.

Dithering Algorithms — Which to Use When

Dithering converts a grayscale image into a pattern of black-and-white dots that, when engraved, create the illusion of gray tones. Choosing the right algorithm for your material and image type is critical.

Floyd-Steinberg

Error DiffusionBest for PhotosMost Popular

Distributes quantization error to neighboring pixels, producing a natural-looking dot pattern with no obvious directional artifacts. Best all-around choice for photographs on wood, leather, and slate. The dots are randomized enough that small imperfections in engraving speed don't create visible banding. Start here for any photo job. Works especially well on darker woods like walnut and cherry.

Jarvis / Jarvis-Judice-Ninke

Error DiffusionMore DetailSlower to Compute

Similar to Floyd-Steinberg but spreads error over a wider neighborhood, producing slightly finer detail and smoother gradients. Use for high-resolution photos where you want maximum tonal range. The increased detail can look slightly noisy on rough materials but is excellent on smooth acrylic, painted tiles, or powder-coated metal. Computation time is slightly longer but still fast.

Stucki

Error DiffusionClean & SharpGood for Portraits

Another error-diffusion variant with a different weighting matrix that tends to produce sharper edges compared to Floyd-Steinberg. Excellent for portraits and face engraving where you want fine detail in hair and facial features. The slight sharpening effect helps on light-colored woods (pine, maple) where the laser contrast is lower. Many engravers consider Stucki the best algorithm for portrait work.

Newsprint / Halftone

Ordered DitherArtistic EffectHigh Contrast Images

Creates circular/dot halftone patterns similar to newspaper printing. Best for high-contrast logos and artwork where you want a visible halftone aesthetic rather than photorealistic reproduction. Not ideal for photos — the regular dot pattern looks artificial. Great for stamp plates, artistic prints, and any design where the dithering pattern itself is part of the aesthetic. Also works well on black anodized aluminum.

2D / Diagonal Line Dithering

Line BasedFast ExecutionHigh Contrast

Creates diagonal or crossed line patterns instead of dots. Best for logos, text, and simple graphics with clear black/white areas. Very fast to engrave because the laser moves in long continuous strokes. Produces a hatching/crosshatch appearance that can look artistic on certain materials. Not suitable for photographs where you need smooth gradients — the line pattern becomes very obvious.

1-Bit (No Dithering / Threshold)

BinaryClean LinesLogos & Line Art

Simple threshold: pixels above 50% brightness = no laser, below 50% = full laser. Best for pure black-and-white logos, QR codes, barcodes, and line art. Zero dithering artifacts. If your image is already black-and-white (not grayscale), always use this mode. Produces the sharpest possible lines and the fastest G-code generation.

Line Distance (DPI / Resolution)

Line Distance is the gap between engraving lines in millimeters. This directly controls the effective DPI of your engrave:

0.5mm~50 DPIFast fills, draftsFastest
0.25mm~100 DPIText, basic logosFast

For a typical 100×100mm photo on wood at 0.1mm line distance, expect 30–60 minutes of engrave time at 3000mm/min. At 0.15mm, it drops to 20–30 minutes with only a minor quality reduction. Match line distance to your laser spot size — using 0.05mm lines on a laser with a 0.15mm spot just wastes time with no quality gain.

Engraving Direction

Horizontal (Left-Right) ↔

Default. Most efficient for most images. The Y axis makes small steps while X does all the work. Good for landscape-oriented images. Some machines show mechanical resonance in horizontal that vertical doesn't — experiment with both.

Vertical (Up-Down) ↕

X makes small steps, Y does all the work. Better for portrait-oriented images. Can reduce Y-axis banding artifacts visible on some machines. Try this if horizontal engraves show regular horizontal stripes.

Diagonal (45°)

Engraves at 45 degrees. Distributes mechanical vibration across both axes simultaneously. Can give the smoothest result on machines with slight axis vibration, at the cost of more complex G-code and slightly longer job times.

Bidirectional vs Unidirectional

Bidirectional engraves in both left-to-right and right-to-left passes (faster). Unidirectional only engraves in one direction (slower, potentially more accurate). If you see a left-right line shift (alternating dark/light lines), enable "Line Shift Compensation" in Settings rather than switching to unidirectional.

Pre-Processing Your Image for Best Results

In GIMP (Free) or Photoshop Before Importing

  • Convert to grayscale — LaserGRBL converts color internally but doing it manually first gives more control
  • Increase contrast by 20–40% — Laser engravings have less tonal range than ink, so boosting contrast compensates
  • Sharpen slightly — Unsharp mask at 50% amount, 0.5 radius helps edge definition
  • Remove background — Use PNG with transparent background so only the subject engraves
  • Check minimum line width — Any line thinner than 0.15mm at your output size may not engrave visibly
India Use Case: Business Cards & Name Boards

For wood business card engraving (a popular small business in India), use: PNG image at 600 DPI input resolution, 1-Bit Threshold algorithm for clean text, 0.1mm line distance, horizontal direction, 3000mm/min speed, 80% power on 4mm ply. The Sculpfun S9 can do 30–40 cards per hour with this setup. Bamboo visiting card blanks from Robu.in or local stationery shops work beautifully.

Vector & SVG Import

Vectors work fundamentally differently from raster images in LaserGRBL. Instead of converting pixels to dots, vectors tell the laser to trace precise mathematical paths. This means perfectly sharp lines at any size, faster cutting jobs, and cleaner outlines — but requires understanding how LaserGRBL handles SVG specifically.

How LaserGRBL Handles Vectors vs Images

Raster Image Workflow

Image → Dithering → Horizontal scan lines → G-code with S-value modulated per pixel column → Laser sweeps back and forth filling the whole area

Time is proportional to the area of the bounding box, not the complexity of the design.

Vector SVG Workflow

SVG paths → G-code lines following path curves → Laser traces outlines and/or fills → Result has mathematically perfect edges at any scale

Time is proportional to path length, not bounding area. Sparse designs are much faster.

For cutting operations (cutting out shapes from thin material), vectors are essential — you need to trace the cut line exactly, not raster-fill the whole area. For engraving fills, you can choose to either raster-fill a vector shape (like engraving) or trace outline paths (like scoring).

Importing SVG Files

  1. Go to File → Open File and select a .svg file. LaserGRBL uses its internal SVG parser — it supports basic SVG path data but NOT all SVG features.
  2. The Vector Import Dialog appears (different from the raster wizard). Here you choose how each path group should be handled.
  3. For each layer/group in the SVG, choose: Line Mode (trace the path outline) or Fill Mode (raster-fill the enclosed area like an engraving).
  4. Set speed and power for each mode. Cutting lines typically use lower speed and higher power than fill engraving.
  5. Set output size in mm. LaserGRBL respects SVG document dimensions but you can override.
  6. Click OK. Preview shows the vector paths as thin colored lines.
LaserGRBL SVG Limitations
  • No gradient support — Gradient fills are ignored or rendered as solid fills
  • No embedded rasters — SVG files with embedded PNG/JPG images inside won't show the raster part
  • Basic path support — Complex SVG filters, masks, and effects are ignored
  • No text rendering — SVG text elements must be converted to paths before importing

Speed & Power Settings

Speed and power are the two most critical variables in laser engraving and cutting. Too fast = not enough energy (light or incomplete burn). Too slow = over-burn (charring, fire risk, melted acrylic). This tab gives you complete reference tables, explains the GRBL S-value system, and shows you how to build material test grids so you never guess again.

Understanding GRBL S-Values vs Percentage

In GRBL, laser power is controlled by the S parameter in G-code commands:

G1 X50 Y0 F3000 S800   ; move right 50mm at 3000mm/min, laser at S=800

The S-value ranges from 0 (laser off) to the value set by $30 (maximum power, usually 1000). LaserGRBL shows you a percentage (0–100%) in its UI and converts it to S-values internally using: S = percentage × ($30 / 100)

$30 = 1000 (most machines)

  • 100% power = S1000
  • 80% power = S800
  • 50% power = S500
  • 20% power = S200
  • 1% power = S10

$30 = 255 (old machines)

  • 100% power = S255
  • 80% power = S204
  • 50% power = S127
  • 20% power = S51
  • 1% power = S2
Critical: $32=1 Laser Mode Must Be Enabled

When $32=1 (laser mode), GRBL modulates the laser power dynamically as the head accelerates and decelerates. This prevents over-burning at corners and line starts where the head slows down. With $32=0 (spindle mode), the laser stays at constant power regardless of speed — causing burned corners and ignoring S-values during rapid changes. Always verify $32=1 before any job.

Feed Rate Explained

Feed rate (F value in G-code) is the movement speed of the laser head in mm/min. In LaserGRBL's image conversion dialog, you set "Speed" — this becomes the F value. The relationship between speed and power determines energy density:

  • Higher speed + higher power = same total energy, but less heat soak (better for thin materials, reducing burn edges)
  • Lower speed + lower power = same total energy, but more heat soak (worse for crisp edges but deeper in thick material)
  • Effective Engraving: Find the combination that marks the material without charring surrounding areas

Your machine's maximum reliable feed rate depends on the stepper motor drivers and $110/$111 GRBL settings. Most budget diode laser machines handle 3000–6000mm/min for engraving, 300–800mm/min for cutting reliably. Exceeding $110/$111 causes GRBL to automatically cap the speed without warning — your actual speed may be lower than requested.

Building a Material Test Grid

Never guess settings for a new material. Build a test grid first. LaserGRBL has a built-in Create Calibration Grid tool:

  1. Go to Tools → Create Calibration Grid (or equivalent in your version — some call it "Test Grid Generator").
  2. Set the X axis to represent Speed: e.g., 1000, 2000, 3000, 4000mm/min (4 columns).
  3. Set the Y axis to represent Power: e.g., 40%, 60%, 80%, 100% (4 rows).
  4. Set cell size (e.g., 10×10mm). Total grid: 40×40mm on a scrap piece of your target material.
  5. Run the grid. Each cell engraves with a unique speed/power combination. Read the results to find the ideal cell — darkest without charring the edges.
  6. Save the optimal settings per material. Use LaserGRBL's "Material Library" feature (if available in your version) or simply keep a note — many users stick a hand-written settings card to their machine.
Test Grid Pro Tips
  • Run the grid on the same batch of material you'll use for the final job — material quality varies significantly between batches
  • For cutting tests, use line mode with a simple rectangle in each cell and check for clean edge, minimal char, and full penetration
  • Photograph your test grid and store it with your LaserGRBL project file for future reference
  • Re-run a mini test grid (4 cells) whenever you get a new material shipment, even if it's the "same" product
  • Laser power decreases over time as the diode ages — your settings from 6 months ago may need 10–15% more power today

Saving Settings Per Material

LaserGRBL does not have a built-in material profile library like LightBurn, but there are practical workarounds:

  • Save converted G-code files — After optimizing settings for a material/design combination, save the G-code via File → Save G-code. You can re-load and re-run it without going through the wizard again.
  • Custom button presets — Create toolbar buttons (Edit → Manage Buttons) that run a G-code snippet setting your favorite speed/power values for common materials. One click to switch "Wood Mode" or "Leather Mode."
  • Spreadsheet or notepad — Keep a simple settings log: Material | Thickness | Speed | Power | Passes | Result. Many serious users maintain this as a shared Google Sheet.
  • Filename conventions — Name project files like logo_birch3mm_3000mmpm_75pct.lgcode so settings are visible in the filename without opening the file.

G-Code & Advanced Features

What is G-Code?

G-Code is the universal language of CNC machines and laser engravers. It is a series of plain text instructions that tell the machine where to move (X, Y, Z coordinates), at what speed, and when to turn the laser on/off. When you import an image in LaserGRBL and configure settings, LaserGRBL generates G-Code automatically and streams it to your machine line by line over USB.

You don't need to write G-Code manually for most jobs, but understanding it gives you more control — especially for troubleshooting, resuming after power cuts (very common in India!), and creating automation macros.

Resume After Power Cut — India Power Cut Tip

India Reality: Power cuts (load shedding) happen without warning in many Indian cities, especially in summer. A job that gets interrupted mid-way can waste your material. Here is how to protect yourself:
  1. Always export the G-Code file before starting a long job (save as .nc file)
  2. When power returns, open the saved .nc file in LaserGRBL
  3. In the G-Code viewer, scroll to approximately where the job was interrupted (you can see the burn marks on the material)
  4. Right-click on the line → "Start from here"
  5. Carefully realign your material to the exact same position using the origin marks you made
  6. Resume the job from that line
Prevention: A basic UPS (₹2,000–4,000) that powers your laser control board and computer for 15–30 minutes can save a job during short cuts. The laser module itself draws too much power for most small UPS units, so the laser will shut off but you can save your G-Code position.

Multi-Pass via G-Code

For deep cuts, multiple passes at the same Z height are better than one slow pass (which burns excessively). In LaserGRBL, set the "Line Pass" count before engraving. Each pass re-runs the entire G-Code, allowing the cut to go deeper without charring. For 6mm plywood cuts, 5–7 passes at 100% power / 400 mm/min is better than 1 pass at 50 mm/min.

Homing and Work Coordinates

LaserGRBL uses a simple work coordinate system. When you click "Set Origin" in LaserGRBL, it sets the current head position as X=0, Y=0 for that job. No physical homing (limit switches) is required for most diode laser work — just place your material, position the head at the desired start point, and click Set Origin.

If your machine has limit switches: run $H to home first, then move to your material and set work origin. This allows repeatable positioning across sessions.

Speed Override During Job

LaserGRBL supports real-time speed and power override during a running job using the sliders in the main interface. You can increase or decrease speed by ±10–50% to see effect in real time. This is useful for test engraving — start at higher speed, slow down if too light. Note: changes are temporary for that job only.

Camera & Overlay

LaserGRBL Camera Feature Overview

LaserGRBL has a built-in camera overlay feature that lets you see your material through a webcam and position your design on top of it visually. This is useful for:

  • Precise placement on pre-made objects (wallets, coasters, pens)
  • Avoiding grain patterns or knots in wood
  • Centering designs without measuring
  • Second-pass alignment for multi-layer burns
Limitation: LaserGRBL's camera overlay is functional but basic compared to LightBurn's camera system. LightBurn offers automatic lens distortion correction and precise calibration. LaserGRBL's camera is suitable for approximate visual placement. For sub-millimeter precision, use physical jigs instead.

Setting Up Camera in LaserGRBL

  1. Connect a USB webcam to your computer. Mount it above the laser work area — at least 30–50cm above for a full view. A flexible arm or overhead mount works best.
  2. In LaserGRBL, go to View → Camera (or click the camera icon in the toolbar)
  3. Select your camera from the dropdown list
  4. A camera window will appear showing the live feed
  5. Overlay your design on the camera feed to visually position it

Recommended webcam: Any 720p USB webcam works. In India, Logitech C270 (₹1,800) or Zebronics USB webcam (₹500–800) are popular options.

Calibrating Camera Position

For the overlay to be accurate, you need to calibrate the camera's field of view against the machine's coordinate system:

  1. Burn 4 corner marks on a piece of cardboard (use a low-power dot at each corner of your work area)
  2. In the camera overlay, click the calibration points to match the burned dots to the machine coordinates
  3. LaserGRBL will then map camera pixels to machine coordinates
  4. Re-calibrate whenever you move the camera
Quick Calibration Method: Use a ruler or known-size template (e.g., a 100mm × 100mm square cut from cardboard). Burn it first, then use the corners as calibration reference points for the camera overlay.

Using Overlay to Position Designs

  1. Place your material on the bed
  2. Open the camera view — you'll see a live image of your material
  3. Import your design in LaserGRBL
  4. Enable the overlay — the design appears as a ghost image on top of the camera feed
  5. Drag the design in LaserGRBL to the desired position on your material
  6. The X/Y coordinates update in real time
  7. Engrave when satisfied with the position

Red Dot Pointer: Using $32=0 to See Beam Path

CAUTION — Eye Safety Critical: The following technique uses the laser at very low power to trace the job path. ALWAYS wear laser safety goggles rated for your laser's wavelength (OD4+ at 450nm for blue diode lasers). NEVER look directly at the beam even at low power.

To preview the job path as a visible trace:

  1. Type $32=0 in the console (disables laser mode — laser is now always on when moving)
  2. In LaserGRBL, set power to 1–3% maximum
  3. Start the job — the laser will trace the path visibly at very low power
  4. Observe the trace path on your material
  5. Cancel the job, adjust position if needed
  6. CRITICAL: Type $32=1 before doing the real job — if you forget this, the laser burns at full power during all movement, destroying your material

Many users skip this technique and use the "frame" button instead (which moves the head around the bounding box without firing the laser). The frame method is safer and simpler for most positioning tasks.

Use a physical ruler to mark your material's edges and corners, then use the LaserGRBL coordinate readout to position the head at known coordinates. This method is reliable and requires no camera.

Pro Tips

Always Use Shielded USB Cable

The single most common cause of random disconnects and job failures is an unshielded USB cable. The stepper motor PWM signals create electromagnetic interference (EMI) that corrupts USB data. Use a shielded USB-A to USB-B cable (or the appropriate connector for your machine) with a ferrite core clamp. Cost: ₹100–200. This one upgrade eliminates 80% of connection issues. Avoid USB hubs and extenders — connect direct to PC.

Air Assist DIY — Aquarium Pump Method (₹300–500)

Air assist blows a focused stream of air across the laser focal point, which:

  • Clears smoke away from the lens (extends lens life dramatically)
  • Reduces charring on wood (the smoke is what causes the dark halo around burns)
  • Allows faster cutting speeds
  • Reduces fire risk

DIY setup: Buy a small aquarium air pump (₹200–300 from pet shops), connect airline tubing, attach a bent metal tube or 3D-printed nozzle aimed at the laser focus point. Total cost: ₹300–500. This outperforms many commercial air assist kits costing ₹2,000+.

Fire Safety — Non-Negotiable

Fire Safety Rules (India Context):
  • Never leave the machine unattended while running — even for 2 minutes. Go to bathroom? Pause the job.
  • Keep a bucket of dry sand next to your laser. In India, CO2 fire extinguishers are less common in homes. Sand works on any fire and doesn't damage electronics like water does.
  • A basic CO2 fire extinguisher (₹800–1,500) is a worthwhile investment once you're running the machine regularly
  • Do not engrave unknown materials — some plastics release toxic/flammable gases
  • Keep workspace ventilated — open window or use exhaust fan
  • If using an enclosure, ensure it has ventilation — smoke buildup is a fire risk
  • Keep a spray bottle of water nearby for smoldering material (not for electrical fires)

When to Upgrade to Fiber Laser — For SS304 Stamp Plates

The Fiber Laser Decision:

Diode lasers cannot mark bare stainless steel (SS304) — the 450nm blue light reflects off bare metal. Fiber lasers (1064nm infrared) are absorbed by metal and can engrave, mark, and even cut thin metal.

If you're in the stamp plate business or want to engrave metal nameplates, jewelry, cutlery, or industrial parts — you need a fiber laser.

India fiber laser pricing (2024):

  • Budget desktop fiber (20W MOPA): ₹1,80,000–2,50,000 (Chinese brands: Cloudray, Raycus)
  • Mid-range fiber (30W MOPA): ₹3,00,000–4,50,000
  • Professional (50W MOPA): ₹6,00,000–9,00,000

Recommendation: Once your diode laser business consistently earns ₹30,000+/month and you're regularly losing business because you can't do metal engraving — then invest in fiber. Until then, the diode laser has a much broader material range for gifting/craft markets and much lower entry cost.

A fiber laser + diode laser combination covers 95% of all laser engraving requests in India.
