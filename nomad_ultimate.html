Getting Started with Nomad Sculpt
What it is, what you need, and how to set it up for serious work
What is Nomad Sculpt? Nomad Sculpt is a professional-grade 3D sculpting app for iPad and Android tablets. It rivals desktop software like ZBrush for sculpting workflows — dynamic topology, voxel remesh, PBR materials, vertex painting, and STL export for 3D printing. One-time purchase, no subscription (~₹800 on App Store / Play Store).

⚙ Initial Setup
  • Download Nomad Sculpt from App Store or Play Store (~₹800, one-time)
  • Open app → Settings (⚙) → Performance → set Max Polycount to match your device RAM
  • Enable Smooth shading in scene settings for better preview
  • Set Unit System to mm if printing (Settings → Scene → Units)
  • Turn on Lazy mouse in brush settings for smoother strokes
  • Enable Symmetry (X axis) — top toolbar — for all character sculpting
  • Set background to grey — easier to judge form than white or black
🧠 Core Concepts
Polygon Mesh

Your model is made of triangles or quads. More polygons = more detail, but heavier on performance. Nomad manages this with dynamic topology and remesh.

Dynamic Topology (Dyntopo)

Nomad adds and removes polygons automatically as you sculpt. Sculpt anywhere — detail only exists where you paint it. Great for freeform organic work.

Voxel Remesh

Rebuilds the mesh at a uniform resolution — like a 3D grid. Cleans up messy topology mid-sculpt. Destructive but powerful for starting fresh.

Multiresolution

Sculpt at multiple detail levels — rough blocking at low res, fine surface detail at high res. Switch between levels non-destructively.

Symmetry

Mirror sculpt strokes across an axis. Enable X symmetry for characters — every stroke on the left mirrors to the right instantly. Saves 50% of sculpting time.

Masking

Paint a mask to protect areas from sculpting. Masked areas (shown in darker grey) are unaffected by brushes. Essential for partial edits.

PBR Materials

Physically Based Rendering — realistic material properties (roughness, metallic, colour). Nomad renders these in real-time on your tablet — no render farm needed.

Scene Graph

The object list (left panel) — all your objects, groups, and primitives. Tap to select, drag to reorder, use groups to organise complex models.

📐 Setting Up for 3D Printing
1
Set units to millimetres

Settings (⚙) → Scene → Units → Millimetres. Now 1 unit = 1mm — what you sculpt is what you print.

2
Enable grid snapping

Helps keep base flat. Grid → enable in the scene settings. Useful when placing a model on a flat base.

3
Add a reference cube

Add → Box → resize to your target print size (e.g. 32mm tall for 28mm scale miniature). Sculpt your character inside this bounding reference.

4
Check wall thickness regularly

Thin walls (under 0.8mm for resin, 1.5mm for FDM) will fail to print. View → Thickness overlay to visualise thin areas in red.

5
Export as STL or OBJ

Files (📁 icon) → Export → STL or OBJ. Send to slicer via AirDrop, Files app, or cloud storage.

Sculpt Tools Reference
Every brush explained — what it does, when to use it, and how to get the best result
🏗 Volume Building Brushes
🏔ClayBeginner

Builds up volume smoothly — like adding clay. The most-used brush for blocking and building forms. Soft falloff, even distribution.

Use for: body mass, general volume building, blocking out shapes
🧱Clay StripsBeginner

Like Clay but with a flat, strip-like shape. Builds up more pronounced ridges and strips — perfect for muscles and directional forms.

Use for: muscles, raised armour ridges, directional forms
🌊FlattenBeginner

Flattens geometry to the average plane of the brush area. Excellent for creating flat armour surfaces and smoothing broad areas.

Use for: armour plates, flat surfaces, knocking back bumps
⬆InflateBeginner

Pushes geometry outward along normals — puffs up the surface evenly. Hold invert (or enable subtract) to deflate.

Use for: cheeks, belly, inflated surfaces, scars
🔵BlobIntermediate

Spherical volume addition — like pressing a ball into the surface. Creates round bumps and warts. Very strong falloff.

Use for: warts, eyes, bolts, rivets, organic bumps
🌀LayerIntermediate

Builds geometry to a fixed height — won't exceed a set depth limit. Ideal for consistent thin layers like scales or tiling panels.

Use for: fish scales, tile patterns, panel depth consistency
✂ Carving & Cutting Brushes
✏CreaseBeginner

Carves a sharp crease into the surface — the most important detail brush. Creates wrinkles, seams, panel lines, armour joints.

Use for: wrinkles, seams, panel lines, sharp folds in cloth
〰PinchIntermediate

Pulls geometry inward toward the brush stroke centre — sharpens ridges and defines edges. Use subtly.

Use for: sharpening edges, tightening creases, fins
🔪TrimIntermediate

Cuts a flat plane through the mesh — like slicing with a knife. Creates hard flat cuts on the surface. Excellent for hard-surface work.

Use for: cutting flat surfaces, slicing shapes, armour planes
💠Trim (Line)Intermediate

Draw a straight line — everything on one side is trimmed flat. Great for flat base cuts or sharp mechanical cuts.

Use for: cutting model flat for printing base, mechanical edges
🔲Trim (Rectangle)Advanced

Trim a rectangular area flat in one stroke — like milling a slot or recess into the surface.

Use for: panel recesses, slots, mechanical cavities
🔘CavityIntermediate

Sculpts into concave areas only — follows existing cavities and deepens them without affecting raised areas.

Use for: deepening eye sockets, nostrils, panel lines
🖐 Transform & Move Brushes
👆MoveBeginner

Pushes geometry in the direction of your stroke — like pushing clay with a finger. Essential for posing and major form adjustments.

Use for: adjusting proportions, posing, pushing large masses
🌀GrabBeginner

Grabs a region of geometry and moves it as a unit — like picking up a lump of clay. Larger radius = larger grab area.

Use for: pulling out noses, ears, horns from the base mesh
↔Elastic GrabIntermediate

Like Grab but with elastic deformation — surrounding geometry follows the movement smoothly. Great for organic posing.

Use for: natural-feeling pose adjustments, organic deformation
🌀TwistIntermediate

Rotates geometry in a circular motion around the brush centre. Creates spirals, twisted cloth, and rotating deformations.

Use for: twisted cloth, rope texture, spiral horns
〰Snake HookIntermediate

Pulls geometry out in the stroke direction, creating tentacle-like extrusions from the surface. Dynamic topology recommended.

Use for: tentacles, horns, spines, tails, spikes
⚙Transform (Gizmo)Beginner

Standard move/rotate/scale gizmo for precise object-level transformation. Switch between local and world coordinates.

Use for: placing objects, scaling primitives, precise positioning
🎨 Utility Brushes
💧SmoothBeginner

Softens and blurs geometry — removes lumps, brush marks, and rough detail. The second most-used brush after Clay. Hold Shift to smooth temporarily from any other brush.

Hold Shift on any brush to get temporary Smooth
🟤MaskIntermediate

Paint areas to protect from sculpting. Masked areas turn dark grey and are immune to all brush effects. Invert mask, clear mask, or grow/shrink mask options available.

Use for: protecting finished areas while sculpting nearby
🔲Face GroupAdvanced

Paint coloured face groups to define regions of the mesh. Used with masking and for material assignment zones.

Use for: defining UV zones, material regions, selective remesh
🎨PaintBeginner

Vertex colour painting directly on the mesh. Set colour, roughness, and metallic value per vertex. Exports with GLB format.

Use for: adding colour and material variation to sculpts

Topology & Remeshing
Managing polygon density — the most important technical skill in Nomad
Why topology matters: Too few polygons = blocky, can't add detail. Too many = slow performance, laggy sculpting. The right density for each stage of the workflow is the key skill that separates smooth workflows from frustrating ones.
🔺 Dynamic Topology (Dyntopo)

Enable in the top toolbar → Topology → Dynamic. Nomad adds and removes triangles as you sculpt — detail only exists where you paint.

When to Use

  • Freeform organic sculpting — creatures, characters
  • When you don't know the final form yet
  • Pulling out tentacles, spines, complex shapes with Snake Hook
  • Early blocking where you need flexibility

Settings

  • Detail size: Controls triangle size — smaller = more detail. Set relative to your brush size.
  • Method: Subdivide (adds) / Collapse (removes) / Subdivide+Collapse (both)
  • Constant detail: Uniform density everywhere vs brush-relative density

Pros & Cons

ProCon
Works anywhere on the meshIrregular triangles
No pre-planning neededCan get very heavy fast
Detail exactly where neededHarder to remesh later
Great for organic formsNot ideal for hard-surface
Tip: Use Dyntopo for blocking only. Once happy with the form, voxel remesh to get clean even topology, then continue detailing with multiresolution.
🔲 Voxel Remesh

Topology → Remesh (Voxel). Rebuilds the entire mesh at a uniform resolution — like filling a 3D grid with the shape of your model.

When to Use

  • After boolean operations (union/subtract) to clean up
  • Switching from Dyntopo blocking to detail sculpting
  • When topology has become a tangled mess
  • Before adding Multiresolution levels

Voxel Size Guide

StageVoxel SizeResult
Rough block3–5mmVery low poly, fast
Mid detail1–2mmGood for muscles/cloth
Fine detail0.3–0.8mmWrinkles, pores
Extreme detail0.1–0.2mmHeavy — Pro iPads only

Important Notes

  • Destructive — replaces all topology. Undo available immediately after.
  • Removes vertex paint — bake vertex colours before remeshing if needed
  • Closes holes — remesh fills any holes in your mesh (good for printing)
  • Smoothing option — enable smooth after remesh to remove voxel stepping
Save before remesh! Always save a copy before voxel remesh — it's destructive and high voxel counts can be very heavy.
📐 Multiresolution (Multires)

Topology → Multires. Add subdivision levels non-destructively — sculpt at high res, go back to low res, changes propagate both ways.

Workflow

  1. Block out with Dyntopo or primitives
  2. Voxel remesh to get clean even topology
  3. Add Multires → Subdivide once (level 1)
  4. Sculpt medium detail (muscles, cloth folds)
  5. Subdivide again (level 2)
  6. Sculpt fine detail (wrinkles, skin pores, cloth weave)
  7. Go back to level 1 to adjust major forms if needed

Level Guide

  • Level 0 (base): Base cage — major proportion adjustments
  • Level 1: Muscles, major folds, armour plates
  • Level 2: Cloth texture, skin, secondary forms
  • Level 3: Pores, fine wrinkles, microdetail
  • Level 4+: iPad Pro M2+ only — extreme detail
Best of both worlds: Block with Dyntopo → Voxel remesh → Multires for detailing. This is the professional workflow.
✂ Booleans

Scene panel → select objects → long press → Boolean. Combine, subtract, or intersect meshes.

OperationResultUse For
UnionMerge two objects into one meshCombining body parts, attaching accessories
DifferenceSubtract one from the otherCutting holes, carving recesses, chamfers
IntersectKeep only the overlapping volumeCreating complex intersecting shapes
After any boolean operation, Voxel Remesh the result to get clean topology before continuing to sculpt.
📉 Decimate (Reduce Polycount)

Topology → Decimate. Reduces polygon count while preserving shape — essential before exporting to a slicer.

  • Set target % — 50% decimate keeps half the polygons with minimal visual loss
  • Preview the result before committing
  • Aim for under 1M triangles for most slicers (Chitubox, Lychee handle up to 5M)
  • Decimate after all sculpting is finished — do not decimate and then continue sculpting
  • Use Smooth Normals after decimating to reduce shading artifacts
Sculpting Techniques
How to actually sculpt — workflows, approaches, and technique breakdowns
🏗 Blocking Workflow — The Right Order
Golden rule: Always work from big forms to small detail. Never add fine detail before the big shapes are correct. Detail on bad proportions is wasted work.
1
Start from a primitive

Add a Sphere for organic forms (heads, bodies), Box for hard-surface (armour, weapons). Enable X symmetry.

2
Block the overall silhouette

Use Move/Grab and Clay to establish the overall mass and proportions. Check from Front, Side, and Top views. Spend 30% of your time here.

3
Secondary forms

Add the big secondary shapes — chest muscles, shoulder blades, jaw, brow. Still use large brushes. Don't crease anything yet.

4
Voxel remesh to clean up

After major forms are established, remesh for clean topology. Choose voxel size for the next level of detail.

5
Add Multiresolution — Level 1

Subdivide once. Define muscles, cloth folds, armour panel shapes. Smooth between forms.

6
Subdivide — Level 2

Fine surface detail: wrinkles, cloth texture, skin pores, stitching, filigree. Use Crease, Pinch, and small Clay brushes.

7
Final pass

Check from all angles. Rotate around and spot errors. Add missing details. Paint vertex colours if needed.

👤 Human Head Sculpting

Proportions (28mm scale)

  • Head height = 1/7 to 1/8 of total body
  • Eyes sit at the midpoint of the head height
  • Nose = 1/3 from eye line to chin
  • Mouth = 1/3 from nose to chin
  • Eye width = 1 eye-width between eyes
  • Ear = between eye line and nose line

Step-by-Step Head Block

  1. Start with UV Sphere — squash to egg shape (wider at top)
  2. Move front face forward slightly — cheekbones, brow ridge
  3. Push eye sockets in with Cavity or Crease
  4. Pull nose out with Grab
  5. Define jaw edge — flatten the chin
  6. Add ears as separate spheres, boolean union
  7. Crease the nasolabial folds, brow line
  8. Add lip line with thin Crease brush
⚔ Hard-Surface Sculpting (Armour, Weapons)

Key Brushes for Hard-Surface

  • Flatten — the most important brush. Makes true flat planes.
  • Trim (Line) — cuts sharp flat edges across the mesh
  • Crease — sharp panel lines and bevels
  • Clay + Flatten — build up then flatten: clean panels
  • Pinch — sharpen edges after flattening

Hard-Surface Workflow

  1. Start from Box primitive for armour panels
  2. Trim the outer silhouette with Trim (Line)
  3. Flatten panel faces one by one
  4. Crease the panel line edges
  5. Pinch the edges to sharpen them
  6. Add rivets — add small Sphere primitives, boolean Union
  7. Add embossed details with Clay strips
For extreme hard-surface (perfect 90° angles, parametric shapes), model in Shapr3D or Fusion 360, export as OBJ/STL, import into Nomad, then add organic surface detail on top.
👘 Cloth & Fabric Techniques

Cloth Sculpting Rules

  • Fabric folds where it is pinched (belt, shoulder, knee)
  • Folds hang down from pinch points by gravity
  • Compressed folds: small, tight, many (bunched fabric)
  • Stretched folds: long, smooth, few (taut fabric)
  • Study real fabric — drape a cloth over your hand and look at the folds

Cloth Brush Technique

  1. Establish cloth panel shapes with Clay
  2. Smooth broad areas — cloth should be smooth between folds
  3. Use Crease to carve in main fold lines (pinch-point to edge)
  4. Clay Strips to build up fold ridges
  5. Smooth the top of each ridge — round top, sharp base
  6. Add fine cloth texture with Stamp (use fabric alpha) or fine Crease
Vertex Painting & Materials
Colour your sculpt with vertex paint and set up PBR materials for realistic renders
Vertex painting in Nomad stores colour on mesh vertices — not a texture map. It exports with GLB and renders beautifully in Nomad's real-time PBR renderer. For 3D printing, vertex paint is just for visualisation — the printer only sees geometry.
🎨 Vertex Paint Basics
  • Select Paint tool from left toolbar
  • Tap the colour swatch (right panel) to pick colour
  • Roughness slider: 0 = mirror/glossy, 1 = fully matte
  • Metallic slider: 0 = non-metal, 1 = pure metal
  • Intensity: opacity of the paint stroke
  • Wet edge: blends colour at stroke edges naturally
  • Use Fill to flood fill an entire object
  • Use Smooth with paint active to blend colours
Before painting: You need sufficient polygon density — vertex colours look pixelated on low-poly meshes. Subdivide or remesh to at least level 2 before painting detail.
🔮 PBR Materials

Materials → tap any object's material slot to open the material editor.

  • Base Colour: The main diffuse colour of the surface
  • Roughness: How rough or smooth the surface is (0=glass, 1=chalk)
  • Metallic: Whether the surface acts like a metal (0=plastic, 1=chrome)
  • Opacity: Transparency of the material
  • Emission: Glow — makes the surface emit light
  • Normal Map: Import a normal map for surface detail without extra polygons
  • Matcap: Switch to matcap mode for a classic ZBrush-style clay preview
🖼 Matcaps

Matcaps (Material Captures) simulate lighting in a single texture — great for sculpting as they show form clearly without needing to set up lights.

Clay (warm)

Standard sculpting view — shows all form clearly. Use this while sculpting 90% of the time.

Metal

Shows reflective metal surface — useful for checking hard-surface geometry.

Skin

Soft subsurface scattering simulation — great for evaluating character faces.

Custom matcaps

Import your own matcap .png files — download ZBrush matcaps (most are compatible).

Lighting & Scene Rendering
Create stunning renders directly in Nomad — no desktop software needed
💡 Light Types
  • Directional light — parallel rays, like sunlight. Main key light for most renders.
  • Point light — radiates in all directions from a point. Good for lamps, fires, OSL.
  • Spot light — cone of light. Dramatic cinematic lighting.
  • HDRI environment — 360° image-based lighting. Most realistic. Load from Files.
Best setup: One strong directional key light from upper-left + HDRI for ambient fill. This gives a cinematic render in Nomad with minimal setup.
🌐 HDRI Lighting

Scene → Environment → Load HDRI. Download free HDRIs from polyhaven.com — save to Files app, then load into Nomad.

  • Exposure: Brightness of the environment light
  • Blur: How soft the reflections are
  • Rotation: Rotate the HDRI to control main light direction
  • Show background: Toggle whether the HDRI shows in render or just lighting
📸 Screenshots & Renders

Taking a Great Screenshot

  1. Set background to a complementary colour or load HDRI
  2. Enable Post-process → turn on Ambient Occlusion (AO) — darkens crevices for depth
  3. Enable Depth of Field — blurs background for cinematic look
  4. Enable Bloom if you have glowing emission materials
  5. Set resolution to 4× in screenshot settings for a high-res output
  6. Tap the camera icon → Save to Photos

Post-Process Effects

  • Ambient Occlusion (AO): Darkens where surfaces are close together — adds instant depth
  • Depth of Field (DoF): Blurs objects away from focus — cinematic feel
  • Bloom: Glow around bright/emissive areas
  • Tone mapping: Adjusts overall colour/contrast curve
  • Vignette: Darkens edges of the frame slightly
  • Chromatic Aberration: Colour fringing — photographic lens effect
3D Printing Workflow
From finished Nomad sculpt to physical model — complete pipeline
✅ Pre-Export Checklist
1
Check scale in mm

Scene → Units → mm. Verify your model dimensions match the intended print size. A 28mm miniature should be ~28mm tall. Adjust scale using the Transform gizmo.

2
Check for holes in the mesh

Topology → Remesh (Voxel) — this closes all holes automatically. Or check with wireframe view for any obvious gaps in the surface.

3
Check wall thickness

View → Thickness. Red areas are too thin to print reliably. Use Inflate brush to thicken thin areas (fingers, weapon blades, spines). Min: 0.8mm resin, 1.5mm FDM.

4
Merge all objects

Select all objects → Boolean Union (or just Voxel Remesh all) → single merged mesh. Slicers prefer a single mesh.

5
Decimate

Topology → Decimate to 50–70% if the model is over 5M triangles. Slicers struggle with very heavy files. Most printable detail survives 50% decimation.

6
Export STL

Files → Export → STL. Name the file clearly (character_v3_28mm.stl). This goes into your slicer app.

Pro Tips & Advanced Workflow
The tricks that separate professional results from amateur work
⚡ Speed & Efficiency Tips
Always use symmetry

Enable X symmetry for any character from the very first stroke. Turn it off only for unique asymmetric details at the end.

Save versions, not just saves

Files → Save As with a version number (character_v01, v02). Never overwrite your only save. Nomad files include undo history.

Use reference images

Top toolbar → Reference → load a reference image that stays on screen. Split view between reference and sculpt. Don't sculpt from imagination alone.

Rotate constantly

Check your model from all angles every 5 minutes. Silhouette mistakes only visible from one angle. Turn off symmetry temporarily to check the back.

Lazy Mouse for clean lines

Enable Lazy Mouse in brush settings. It follows your stroke with a delay — dramatically smoother lines. Essential for Crease and panel lines.

Work in separate objects

Keep head, body, armour, weapons as separate objects in the scene graph. Easier to move, rescale, and replace parts. Merge only for final export.

Use the Trim brush early

Trim (Line) brush early in blocking gives you flat planes to build detail on. Don't try to flatten with Clay — use the dedicated Flatten and Trim tools.

Mask before risky moves

Before grabbing a large area or doing a major move, mask the parts you want to stay fixed. Prevents accidental deformation of finished areas.

Pipe tool for cables

Use Tube primitive for cables, pipes, tentacles, tails. Set control points along the path. Boolean Union into the main mesh when positioned correctly.

Stamp with alpha brushes

Brush settings → Alpha → import a texture. Stamp it onto the surface for instant texture patterns — scales, chainmail, fabric weave, hex patterns.

Brush falloff matters

Gaussian falloff = soft, organic. Constant = hard, even. Change falloff in brush settings to completely change how a brush behaves — experiment.

Post-process for screenshots only

Turn off AO and DoF while sculpting (performance hit). Only enable them when taking the final screenshot. Assign this to a quick-access button.

🎯 Miniature-Specific Tips (28mm Scale)
28mm scale reality check: At 28mm height, most surface detail below 0.5mm will not print clearly on resin printers. Don't waste hours sculpting microscopic detail that disappears in print. Focus on silhouette and forms visible at arm's length.

What Prints Well

  • Strong silhouette with interesting shapes
  • Recessed panel lines (0.3mm+ depth)
  • Raised rivets, bolts (0.5mm+ diameter)
  • Cloth folds with 0.5mm+ relief
  • Eyes with defined socket and brow ridge
  • Text if 1mm+ letter height

What Doesn't Print Well

  • Fingers thinner than 1mm (merge or simplify)
  • Fabric textures under 0.3mm relief
  • Pores, skin texture (paint it instead)
  • Horizontal overhangs over 45° without supports
  • Weapon blades thinner than 0.8mm (they snap)
  • Hollow spaces without drainage holes

📈 Learning Path
1
Week 1–2: Learn the interface and basic brushes

Sculpt simple objects — a rock, a sword, a simple helmet. Learn Clay, Smooth, Move, Crease. Practice undo/redo, camera controls, symmetry.

2
Week 3–4: Complete a simple head sculpt

Follow a reference. Focus on proportions, not detail. Practise the big-to-small approach. Don't add fine wrinkles before the nose shape is right.

3
Month 2: Full character block-out

Full body at low-medium resolution. Get proportions, pose, and silhouette right. Don't finish any part until the whole is blocked out.

4
Month 3: Add armour and accessories

Hard-surface on top of organic base. Learn boolean workflow. Practise panel lines and flat surfaces with Flatten/Trim/Crease.

5
Month 4+: Export and print your first model

Full pipeline: sculpt → fix → scale → export → slice → print → clean → paint. The full loop from digital to physical is the real milestone.
